# Ecclesiastes 1 — *Kôhelet, jedôn peatükk*

*A translation of Ecclesiastes 1:2–9 — the wisdom register, the deep-abstraction pole. This text was
almost entirely sayable on existing canon; the whole chapter funnelled to a single word, **vanity**,
which the founder carved from the sea (§137). Uncannily on-theme for a maritime, cyclical culture: the
rivers run to the sea and the sea is never full, the wind circles and returns. Metrolect standard,
elevated register. The Preacher (Qoheleth) reads as **the Teacher** (*talçāji*).*

> **On "vanity."** English fuses two concepts here. Ecclesiastes means **futility, emptiness, vapour**
> (Hebrew *hevel*) — *not* conceit (that is **liighambô** "too many teeth," §136). Nelôxi keeps them
> apart: this chapter's word is **merjälg** — literally *a wake on the sea*, the trace the water closes
> behind you as if you were never there. All labour, and the sea heals over. It pairs with **merjagô**
> "fate" — both drawn from the same water: what the sea deals you, and what it erases.

## The chapter

> **2.** Merjälg, merjälg! Kôg um merjälg.
> *Vanity, vanity! All is vanity.* *(lit. "a wake, a wake — all is a wake the sea closes.")*
>
> **3.** Mis marginôl um inimôle täs tȫst, päikü al?
> *What profit has a person from all their labour, under the sun?*
>
> **4.** Jedôn pôlvô lǟdô, uus pôlvô tulē; agā mā um igävē.
> *One generation goes, a new one comes; but the earth is forever.*
>
> **5.** Päikü tõusô ja lōjū, ja tulē jällü sāl, kus tä tõusi.
> *The sun rises and sets, and comes again to where it rose.*
>
> **6.** Tūļ lǟdô sudô, ja pöördô pôjālô; tä lǟdô ümbär ja ümbär, ja tulē jällü.
> *The wind goes south and turns north; round and round it goes, and returns.*
>
> **7.** Kôg jôgôd kōrrô merelä, agā mer äb um tǟs; kus jôgôd tulē, sāl ne tulē jällü.
> *All the rivers run to the sea, yet the sea is never full; where the rivers come from, there they return.*
>
> **8.** Kôg um väsinü; silm äb piisā nǟgômusôk, ja kôrv äb um tǟs kūlômusôk.
> *All is wearisome; the eye is not satisfied with seeing, nor the ear filled with hearing.*
>
> **9.** Mis ūli, se um; ja äb um miskü uus, päikü al.
> *What has been is what shall be; and there is nothing new under the sun.*

---

## Why this text almost translated itself

The chapter's furniture was **already in the language**, because it is *this culture's* furniture: the
sea (*mer*), the rivers (*jôg*), the wind (*tūļ*), the generations (*pôlvô*), forever (*igävē*), the
sun rising and setting (*tõusô / lōjū*). Verse 7 — the rivers to the sea — needed **not one new word.**
A maritime, cyclical, "nothing new under the sun" worldview is the native worldview of a people who
watch the tide give and take. The only true gap was the abstraction on top: *vanity*.

## The one coinage (§137) — vanity as a wake

- **merjälg** "vanity, futility, emptiness" — *mer* "sea" + **jälg** "trace, wake": the wake the sea
  closes behind you; all labour that leaves no mark. The **futility** sense (Eccl. *hevel*), distinct
  from conceit (*liighambô*). New base word: **jälg** "trace, track, footprint; a boat's wake."
- It joins the sea-field's account of the human condition beside **merjagô** "fate" (the sea's
  portion): the sea *deals* you a lot and *erases* your labour — fate and futility, both from the water.

## Grammar the register leaned on (all settled canon)

- **Gnomic present** throughout — timeless truths take the bare present (*tõusô, kōrrô, tulē*), exactly
  as the folktale proverb does.
- **"where"-clauses** stand in for a "place" noun where wanted (*sāl, kus tä tõusi* "there, where it
  rose") — though *paikô* "place" now exists (§135) if a later text wants it.
- **Comitative of the verbal noun** for "satisfied with X-ing": *piisā nǟgômusôk* "satisfied with
  seeing," *tǟs kūlômusôk* "full with hearing."
- **päikü al** "under the sun" — the refrain, a bare postpositional phrase.

**Net:** the whole chapter runs end-to-end on Nelôxi with a single carved word. The wisdom register —
the deep-abstraction pole opposite the harbor ordinance's institutional one — is now open, and it
turned out the language was already most of the way there.
