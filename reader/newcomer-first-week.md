# Vôrô — The Newcomer's First Week

*A code-switching stretch under `college/DAILY-LIFE-BRIEF.md`: the language seen from the
learner's seat. A worker from beyond the Marīsô border arrives on Monday knowing no Nelôxi; the
text follows her seven days and shows **which parts of the language open first and why** — the
three strata are three different onboarding speeds. Narration is metrolect; her own lines are as
broken as they really would be. Canon-only (v5.77); the words the week needed and lacked are
harvested below.*

*The title is the finding: **vôrô** is one canon word with two glosses — "stranger" and "guest."
The week is the distance between them.*

---

**Mōndag — the crust carries her.**

> Tä aribā mōndagôl, tēnol, Marīsôlt. Mytnik dī: "Pāsportô, bidô." Se tä komprē. "Turistô?" —
> "Nē… tȫ." — "Gūd. Velkô!" Esmõ pǟvôl kēļ um lihtsalt: *taksô, busô, otelô, kafē, farmasī* — se
> sônād um vana amīkôd. Tä tēdô veel midagü, agā tä sāmā kôgõl, kôg pǟvô.

> She arrives on Monday, by train, from Marīsô. The customs officer says: "Passport, please."
> That she understands. "Tourist?" — "No… work." — "Fine. Welcome!" On the first day the language
> is easy: *taxi, bus, hotel, coffee, pharmacy* — those words are old friends. She still knows
> nothing, but she gets by everywhere, all day.

**Tīsdag — her own quarry answers back.**

> Tä nǟgô uus mrovôn, blokôl. *Mrovô* — se sônān tä tēdô! Ja turgôl: *drobô, çenô, xapkô, bilet…*
> "Piletô," dī komprāji, ja muigā. Pehā samā sônā. Nabôr, vana mēs, dī gatôl: "Moin!" Tä vastā:
> "Dankô!" Nabôr naurā: "Nē, nē — *moin* tähendä moin. 'Dankô' tulē pǟrast."

> She sees her new flat, in an apartment block. *Mrovô* — that word she knows! And at the market:
> *drobô, çenô, xapkô, bilet…* "Piletô," says the seller, and grins. Almost the same word. The
> neighbor, an old man, calls out in the street: "Moin!" She answers: "Thanks!" The neighbor
> laughs: "No, no — *moin* means hello. 'Thanks' comes later."

**Kesknǟdôl — the partitive wall.**

> Tä komprā kafēt: "Kafē, bidô… ja lēten." Kafēmelk muigā: "*Kôg* lēten? Lētet, vist?" — "Oi. Jā.
> Lētet, bidô." Niimôdõ tä ôpā: **-n** um kôg, **-t** um mõnü. Petīt sônā, agā se tähendä kôgõn.

> She's buying coffee: "Coffee, please… and *lēten*" — the milk, all of it. The counter-hand
> smiles: "*All* the milk? Some milk, probably?" — "Oops. Yes. Some milk, please." That's how she
> learns: **-n** is the whole thing, **-t** is some. A small word, and it means everything.

**Dônôrsdag — the office, in pointing language.**

> Amtôl um ootkôrd, ja ootkôrd um lent. Lopõks: "Vormā, bidô. Nim sīn. Pǟvô sīn." Amtôji parlā
> fluks; tä komprē ainô "papôrd" ja "demā." — "Lent, bidô. Mä komprē ainô petīt." — "Gūd, gūd.
> Esmõks nim, tôl papôrd, tôl demā jällü." Kôv pǟvô. Agā vormā um valmis.

> At the office there's a queue, and the queue is slow. Finally: "The form, please. Name here.
> Date here." The clerk talks fast; she understands only "papers" and "tomorrow." — "Slowly,
> please. I understand only a little." — "Fine, fine. First the name, then the papers, then
> tomorrow again." A hard day. But the form is done.

**Frīdag — work: the crust is half the office.**

> Esmõ tȫpǟvô. Ja lüstä — amtôl kēļ um pehā samā nagu kôgõl: *kontô, fonô, apô, sônaktô,
> konfirmā.* Tä saatā esmõ sônaktôn, ja amīk tȫlt lugē sen: "Gūd! Ôigus!" Sônād nagu *tēdô,
> tȫdô, tõusô* um kôv — agā nupôd ja papôrd parlā samā kēļt kôgõl.

> First workday. And listen — at the office the language is nearly the same as everywhere:
> *account, phone, app, message, confirm.* She sends her first message, and a friend from work
> reads it: "Good! Correct!" Words like *know, work, get up* are hard — but the buttons and the
> paperwork speak the same language everywhere.

**Sônnavend — the glue wins a haggle.**

> Turgôl tä komprā kalāt. "Xeç rā," dī komprāji. — "Xeç? Liig mult!" — "Peñç, kullõ." — "Tri." —
> "Xtiri. Lopõks." — "Nixō… sāli!" Kôg turg naurā — ja tä naurā. Esmõ mäng, esmõ vôitô.

> At the market she buys some fish. "Six rā," says the seller. — "Six? Way too much!" — "Five,
> sweetie." — "Three." — "Four. Final." — "Oh well… deal!" The whole market laughs, and she laughs
> too. First game, first win.

**Sôndag — vôrô, külalü.**

> Vespôrôl nabôr dī: "Tulē, sȫ mēgk." Kōtôl um lǟmi; naisô panā lôunān, läpx kantā. Tä komprē äb
> kôgõn — agā pehā. Ja nabôr dī, lent ja selgô: "Mōndagôl sä ūli **vôrô**. Avuī sä um **külalü**."
> Se tä komprē kôgõn. — "Ja varsü?" tä dī. — "Varsü sä um nabôr." Tä muigā: "Mä ôpā. Lent, lent —
> agā edä."

> In the evening the neighbor says: "Come, eat with us." The house is warm; his wife puts out the
> meal, the child sings. She doesn't understand everything — but almost. And the neighbor says,
> slow and clear: "On Monday you were a **stranger**. Today you are a **guest**." That she
> understands completely. — "And soon?" she says. — "Soon you're a neighbor." She smiles: "I'm
> learning. Slowly, slowly — but onward."

## What the week shows — three strata, three speeds

- **Day 1 is free: the crust.** The international layer (*taksô, otelô, kontô, konfirmā*) is
  intelligible before a single lesson — the language's loan-first design is, functionally, its
  immigration policy. A newcomer is never at zero.
- **Day 2 is her own quarry.** A Slavic-speaking arrival finds her words already inside the
  language (*mrovô, drobô, çenô, xapkô*; *bilet ≈ piletô*) — which stratum a newcomer gets "for
  free" depends on where she's from. A Low German speaker would unlock a different shelf.
- **The wall is morphology, not vocabulary.** The partitive/object contrast (*lēten/lētet*) is
  the first genuinely Nelôxi thing she must acquire — nothing in her language prepares it, and it
  can't be pointed at. The office scene shows the workaround register everyone really uses:
  short nouns, *sīn*, sequence words (*esmõks, tôl*), no subordination.
- **The glue is social currency.** Her haggle is won with function words, not vocabulary —
  *liig mult, nixō, sāli* — the exact inventory of `college/FUNCTION-WORDS.md` §hedges/formulae.
- **The lexicon carries the ethics.** *Vôrô* "stranger/guest" needed no coining; canon already
  refuses to separate the two. The week's arc — vôrô → külalü → nabôr — is spelled entirely in
  attested words.
