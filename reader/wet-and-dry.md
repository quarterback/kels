# Märg ja kuiv — the wet-and-dry field

*A domain showcase, not a wordlist. Nelôxi does not carve wetness into abstract degrees ("wet,
wetter, soaked"); it groups the experience by **what kind of water touched a thing and how it
left** — surface or interior, sea or mud, soaking or weeping or standing or draining. This is a
maritime language: being wet with the sea is not the same as being wet with rain, and it has the
words to say so. Metrolect; every form is canon (v5.77).*

---

## 1 · Where the water is — the core three

The split is **not** about how much water, but **where** it sits:

| Form | Sense | The test |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **märg** | wet | water is visibly *on* it — the surface is wet |
| **mȫistü** | moist, damp, clammy | moisture is *held inside* — cloth, soil, wall, skin, air |
| **kuiv** | dry | ordinarily dry |

> **Mäntlô um märg.** — The coat is wet (rained on, surface soaked).
> **Mäntlô um mȫistü.** — The coat is damp (dried on the outside, still holds wet inside).
> **Mäntlô äb um ainô märg; se um soppô.** — The coat is not merely wet; it is **drenched**.

The point of the second line: a coat can *stop* being **märg** and go on being **mȫistü** for a
day. English needs a clause; Nelôxi needs one word swapped for another.

## 2 · How the water left — the verbs of change

Wetness and dryness are **processes**, and the derivation says who did it:

- **kuivstā** — to dry *of itself*, lose moisture: **Gatô kuivstā.** "The street is drying."
- **kuivtā** — to dry *something*: **Mä kuivtā kǟsôd.** "I dry my hands."
- **kuivtādôt** — dried *on purpose*, by someone: **kuivtādôt kalā** "dried fish."
- **märgstä / märgtā** — the wet-side pair: become wet / wet something.

> **Jôg kuivstī.** — The river dried out (by itself, over the season).
> **kuiv jôg** — a dry riverbed (its present state, however it got there).

So dry skin is not "dry" flatly but a *lack of held moisture*:

> **Mȫistôr äb um nahkôl; nahk um kuiv.** — There is no moisture in the skin; the skin is dry.

## 3 · What kind of water — the compounds carry the culture

This is where the language shows its hand. The compounds don't grade wetness; they name **the
water's origin and craft**:

| Compound | Gloss | The distinction it draws |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **mermärg** | sea-wet | soaked or slick with *seawater* — salt on the skin, wet rope, a deck still slick after the wave has passed |
| **mudmärg** | mud-sodden | sodden with *muddy* water, not clean |
| **luukuiv** | bone-dry | dry to the very core (*luu* "bone") |
| **tūļkuiv** | wind-dried | dried by the *wind* — nets, fish, washing on the line |
| **sōlkuiv** | salt-cured | dried *with salt*, preserved (*sōla* "salt") |

> **kuivtādôt kalā** — dried fish, generally.
> **sōlkuiv kalā** — salt-cured fish, specifically — the thing that keeps for the voyage.

In a plain-land language "wet" is one word. On this coast, **mermärg** is its own idea: the
fisher back from the boats is not *märg*, he is *mermärg* — salt-wet, and everyone knows the
difference by the way it dries.

## 4 · The wider field — soaking, weeping, standing, sinking, emptying

Wetness is not one axis but a **field of actions**, each its own root:

- **sokā** — to soak through, become saturated (neutral; the coarse word is *prōmôr*)
- **nôrgā** — to drip, leak, **weep** water — of a wall, a slow leak, a sodden cloth
- **lomp** — a puddle, standing shallow water · **lompômus** — the puddling of it, standing water gathering
- **vettā** — to immerse, put under water · **vettādôt** — immersed, submerged (physical)
- **sükeldôt** — deeply absorbed, immersed in work or thought (the *figurative* submersion — a
  separate word, because the language keeps the body's soaking apart from the mind's)
- **tühjā** — to drain, empty out · **tühjādôt** — drained, emptied · **nôrgdôt** — leaked away
- **svamm** — sponge (and the root of a small family of idioms, §6)
- **soppô** — drenched, dripping, saturated through: **one strong everyday word**, not "very wet"

## 5 · The field in use — collocations that carry the personality

> **Tä tulī soppô nagu vôrk merelt.**
> *She came in drenched like a net (pulled) from the sea.*
>
> **Kōt um mȫistü ja vaikõ, vandôd nôrgā.**
> *The house is damp and quiet; the walls are weeping (seeping, dripping with damp).*
>
> **Gatô um lompôd tǟs.**
> *The street is full of puddles.*
>
> **Jôg um tühjādôt, agā mā veel mȫistü.**
> *The river is drained, but the ground still holds the damp.*
>
> **Tä um sükeldôt tȫs.**
> *She is immersed in the work.*
>
> **Soppô pǟvô** — a day of relentless wet · **lomp-pǟvô** — a puddle-day, a wet and messy one.

## 6 · The sponge idioms — svamm as a way of seeing

A borrowed object-word (**svamm**, Low German *Swamm*) earns its keep by generating metaphor —
the Nelôxi habit of letting one image name several things:

- **svamm-inim** — a sponge-person: someone who absorbs everything around them
- **svamm-mēl** — a sponge-mind: one that soaks up knowledge (*mēl* "mind")
- **svamm-kōt** — a sponge-house: a damp dwelling that takes in water and never quite dries

---

## The point of view

The distinctive Nelôxi move is *not* to match every English category one-for-one — "wet, damp,
moist, soaked, drenched, sodden" — with a separate root. It is to let wetness become a **field**:
soaking (*sokā*), weeping (*nôrgā*), standing (*lomp*), sinking (*vettā*), holding (*mȫistü*), and
emptying (*tühjā*) — with the sea's own wetness (*mermärg*) marked off from all the rest. That
gives the language a *point of view* on water, not a translated inventory of it.

## The interior-wall word

Coined with this field: **vand** "interior wall, the wall of a room" (Low German *Wand*),
zero-final by §109 like *strānd / nord* — distinct from *valô* "defensive wall, rampart" and
*vantô* "shroud." It lets §5's weeping-walls image stand as written — **vandôd nôrgā** "the walls
are weeping" (plural *vand + -ôd*). (A fuller house-interior batch — ceiling, stairs — would
round out the domain; *etāxô* "floor" already exists.)
