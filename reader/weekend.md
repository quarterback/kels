# Sônnavend ja sôndag — The Weekend

*Daily-life prose stretch II under `college/DAILY-LIFE-BRIEF.md` — the same narrator as
`daily-life-workday.md`, off the clock: errands and money, the house, the beach, guests and music,
and the slow Sunday. Play is where a language shows its warmth registers; errands are where it
shows its numbers. Canon-only (v5.77); gaps harvested below.*

---

**1 · Sônnavend, matī — the errands.**

> Sônnavendôl mä magā kuni klôk deveñç — nagu kuning! Tôl turgôlô. Mä komprā pat, vôidôt, kalāt,
> ja kōkôn läpxôlô. Kôg maksā elva rā; mä maksā dünak ja sāmā jedôn drobô. Komprāji tēdô män:
> "Moin! Samā nagu alatü?" — "Samā nagu alatü."

> On Saturday I sleep till nine — like a king! Then to the market. I buy bread, some butter, some
> fish, and a cake for the kid. It all comes to eleven rā; I pay with a twelve and get one back
> in change. The stallholder knows me: "Morning! Same as always?" — "Same as always."

**2 · Kōtôl — the house has its own list.**

> Kōtôl naisô jubā tȫdô: klamôtôd, kambôrd, finestôd. Mä pesā? Nē — se sônān mä äb tēdô veel;
> naisô dī kus, mä panā sāl. Läpx "abī": tä kōrrô ümbär ja kantā. Lôunāl mēg küpsā kōkôn —
> läpx panā liig magūsūt, muidū.

> At home the wife's already at it: the clothes, the rooms, the windows. Do I *pesā*? No — that
> word I don't know yet; she says where, I put it there. The kid "helps": runs around and sings.
> At midday we bake the cake — the kid puts in too much sweet, of course.

**3 · Strāndôl — the sea is free.**

> Pǟrast mēg kǟvô strāndôlô. Sol! Lopõks! Mer um kylm, agā läpx vōigā nagu kalā; mä vōigā hetk
> ja tôl istā rāndôl. Naisô lugē; mä nǟgô lainôd, ja — midagü. Se um se: midagü, kôg pǟvô.
> Sellü tȫ mä volē elkü pǟvô.

> Afterward we walk to the beach. Sun! At last! The sea is cold, but the kid swims like a fish;
> I swim for a moment and then sit on the shore. The wife reads; I watch the waves, and — nothing.
> That's the whole thing: nothing, all day. That's the job I'd take every day.

**4 · Vespôrôl — guests, music, the long table.**

> Vespôrôl tulē külalüd: nabôr, dva amīkôd, ja vana talçāji, ken parlā liig mult agā tēdô kôgõn.
> Mēg sȫ, jōdô olūtôt ja veinôt? — nē, sen sônān mä äb tēdô, mēg jōdô olūtôt. Amīk spēlā
> instrumentôn, naisô kantā vana kançōn, ja lopõks kôg kantā — äb ôigus, agā rôimõ. Läpx dansā
> kuni tä langô. "Skōl!" — "Skōl!" Havs tulē tard.

> In the evening the guests come: the neighbor, two friends, and the old teacher who talks too
> much but knows everything. We eat, we drink beer — and wine? no, that word I don't have either,
> we drink beer. A friend plays, the wife sings an old song, and by the end everyone's singing —
> not *correctly*, but happily. The kid dances till she drops. "Cheers!" — "Cheers!" Good-night
> comes late.

**5 · Sôndag — the slow day.**

> Sôndag um lent pǟvô, ja se um ses tȫ: lent um. Kafē, pa, vaikõ kōt. Mä lugē; läpx pēlā
> pôrandôl? — sīn, maha, kus mēg kǟvô. Pǟrast lôunāt mä kǟvô rāndôlô, vana nabôrôk: tä kalāstā,
> mä istā. "Sāmā miskü?" — "Midagü. Agā se äb um se." Mēg istā kuni vespôr.

> Sunday is a slow day, and that's its job: being slow. Coffee, bread, a quiet house. I read; the
> kid plays on the — floor? — here, down, where we walk. After lunch I walk to the shore with the
> old neighbor: he fishes, I sit. "Getting anything?" — "Nothing. But that's not the point." We
> sit till evening.

**6 · Ja tôl — mōndag.**

> Vespôrôl mä valmistā klamôtôd demāks? — demā eest? — demā. Nixō: mōndag tulē, muidū. Agā nū
> kōt um vaikõ, läpx magā, ja mēg istā veel hetk. "Jôvā vekk?" dī naisô. "Jôvā vekk." Se um kôg,
> ja se piisā.

> In the evening I lay out clothes for — tomorrow? (*for* tomorrow? — tomorrow.) Anyway: Monday's
> coming, of course. But right now the house is quiet, the kid's asleep, and we sit a moment
> longer. "A good week?" says the wife. "A good week." That's all, and it's enough.

## What the weekend exercises

- **Money in dozenal, in passing:** *elva rā; maksā dünak* "pay with a twelve" (comitative *-k*
  as instrument), *jedôn drobô* "one back in change" — the base-12 economy working in a market
  sentence, no ceremony.
- **The narrator hits his own gaps and says so on the page** — *pesā?* (wash/clean), *veinôt?*
  (wine), *pôrandôl?* (floor), *demāks?* (benefactive again) — fluent speakers route around
  missing words with *mä äb tēdô sen sônān* and a paraphrase, which is itself the most realistic
  daily-life register there is. The dodges are the gap-list, dramatized.
- **Warmth registers:** *nagu kral* (like a king), *nagu kalā* (like a fish), *halvõ agā rôimõ*
  (badly but happily), *kuni tä langô* (till she drops), *se piisā* (it's enough) — small-scale
  figurative language from attested words, no coinage.
- **The play lexicon holds:** *spēlā instrumentôn, kantā kançōn, dansā, pēlā, vōigā, kalāstā,
  skōl* — music, dance, swimming, angling, and toasting are all already canon; leisure needed
  almost no new words. What it lacked was **things in the house** (see below).
