# Extinct number systems (historical / dialectal)

These counting systems are no longer standard Nelôxi. They are recorded here as
attested history and survive only in the conservative **inland** dialect, in fixed
expressions (proverbs, rhymes, "how grandmother counts"), and in specialized
Hanseatic trade/legal registers. Standard Nelôxi counts in the Slavic dozenal
system (see coursebook §Numbers).

## Layer 1 — Finnic (oldest; pre-contact)

The original native numerals, now fully archaic outside inland speech:

1 üks · 2 kaks · 3 kolm · 4 nēļa · 5 vīç · 6 kūç · 7 seis · 8 kōtôks · 9 īdôks · 10 kīm
teens -teis · tens -kīm · hundred çent · thousand mīl · ordinals esmī, tôin, -nd

Survives in: inland dialect everyday counting; fossilized set phrases.

## Layer 2 — Low German / Hanseatic (trade register)

Adopted for commerce during the Hanseatic period; a dock-and-ledger register, never
fully everyday. Displaced from general use by the Slavic dozenal system but lingering
in shipping and law.

1 ēn · 2 twē · 3 drē · 4 fēr · 5 fīf · 6 ses · 7 seven · 8 acht · 9 nēgôn · 10 tēn
teens -tein · tens -tig · hundred hunt · thousand dūsônd · ordinals -tô
Trade units: dūtô (dozen 12) · xok (schock 60) · langhunt (long hundred 120)

Note: the fraction words **half** and **dēl** (and *number+dēl*: tri-dēl "third") were
retained from this layer into the modern standard — the trade quarry's one lasting
contribution to canon numbers.

## The fork

Standard Nelôxi replaced its numerals under Marīsô/metroplex economic contact — the
Slavic border numbers became the language of the market, then of everyday life, within
two generations. Numbers are among the domains most readily replaced under contact
pressure; the rest of the language (grammar, phonology, core lexicon) was unaffected.
