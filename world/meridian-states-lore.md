## Core frame

The **Meridian States** are Neloxia's sovereign global-maritime federation: a chain of ports,
island territories, treaty coasts, naval-cable stations, charter cities, and service commonwealths
that grew from the same logic as the European sea-chain, but on an oceanic scale. They are not an
informal sphere and not a merchant company's private estate. They are a constitutional federation
under Neloxian sovereignty, with their own territorial roll, courts of service, fiscal compacts,
local assemblies, and customary authorities.

The federation exists because Neloxia's old continental maritime system eventually needed a global
meridian: Atlantic staging, Mediterranean repair, Esperanzan plantation-and-port settlements,
Indian Ocean logistics, Magau/Macanese finance and mediation, Pacific cable and naval stations,
and Canarian waypoints. The Meridian States turn scattered maritime facts into a governed body.
They extend the old pattern named in the continental files: accumulated jurisdiction rather than
ethnic frontier, accession and compact rather than simple conquest, and ports becoming law-bearing
institutions because shipping, courts, and language travel together.

## Constitutional language status

**Maré** is the Meridian States' language standard and the **fourth full co-standard** of Neloxi,
a peer of Metropolitan Neloxi, Saharannaise, and Congolaise. It is not a dialect, pidgin, service
slang, or colonial shorthand. It has its own seat, its own territorial vocabulary, and its own
archive norms for names and public writing.

Its constitutional status is simple:

- Metropolitan Neloxi remains the older state and court standard.
- Saharannaise remains the Atlantic standard of Atlanta.
- Congolaise remains the Riverine standard of Soninka & Toro.
- **Maré** is the Meridian standard of the overseas federation.

The point is political as much as linguistic. A sovereign global-maritime federation cannot be
administered forever as a footnote to the Baltic archive. Maré gives the Meridian States a public
voice of their own while keeping the shared Neloxi spine. This file deliberately keeps grammar
minimal: grammatical invariance belongs in [`../saharannaise/COMMON-REGISTER.md`](../saharannaise/COMMON-REGISTER.md)
and in the Maré standard documents, especially [`../mare/mare.md`](../mare/mare.md). Place-name
practice belongs in [`../mare/toponymy.md`](../mare/toponymy.md).

## Major territory groups

The requested map overlays correspond to seven broad territory groups. These are administrative
and historical groupings, not claims that every island or coast joined by the same mechanism.
Some entered by old port compact, some by naval station, some by settler charter, some by treaty
protectorate, and some by later federation vote.

### 1. Atlantic group

The Atlantic group is the federation's western ocean face: convoy harbours, deep-water fishing
ports, cable landings, repair islands, weather stations, and family settlements tied to the
Atlanta route. It is the closest Meridian layer to the Saharannaise world, so its vocabulary
carries many sea, wind, nursery, dock, and crew words into wider circulation. It also preserves the
lesson of Atlanta: maritime connection can create a full standard without making every connected
country a domestic province of European Neloxia.

### 2. Mediterranean group

The Mediterranean group links the Adriatic terminal system to western and central Mediterranean
relays. It includes dockyards, quarantine islands, legal-financial ports, customs offices,
ship-registration dependencies, and multilingual pilotage communities. It is the overseas cousin
of the Triste-Krk-Corsica system described in the continental model: law, insurance, repair,
convoy control, and registry practice make the territory as much as land area does.

### 3. Esperanzan group

The Esperanzan group is the federation's creole-settlement belt: plantation ports, free towns,
river mouths, mission schools, dock suburbs, and mixed households where service and migration
produce a dense local lexicon. Its name marks a Meridian tradition of hope-settlements and
charter towns rather than a single ethnicity. Esperanzan Maré is especially strong in domestic,
food, market, music, religious, and labor vocabulary.

### 4. Indian Ocean group

The Indian Ocean group is the long logistics arc: coaling and fuel successors, monsoon ports,
repair basins, pilgrim and contract-labor routes, anti-piracy stations, customs harbours, and
telegraph-to-cable settlements. Its Maré vocabulary tends to enter through navigation, seasonal
shipping, port health, cargo handling, household migration, and maritime law.

### 5. Magau/Macanese group

The **Magau/Macanese** group is the federation's eastern brokerage and finance hinge: a compact
of Macanese, Lusophone, Sinitic, and Neloxian maritime practice. It supplies vocabulary for
notaries, warehouses, gaming houses, clerical finance, brokerage, interpreters, mixed families,
Catholic and temple neighborhoods, and treaty-port etiquette. In the Meridian imagination, Magau
is not merely a port; it is the school of mediation.

### 6. Pacific group

The Pacific group is the oceanic distance layer: cable islands, naval stations, phosphate and
repair dependencies, migrant settlements, weather and cyclone offices, schools for pilots and
wireless operators, and customary island authorities. Its vocabulary often comes from service
life, navigation, weather, kinship-by-adoption, canoe and lagoon practice, church schooling, and
military administration.

### 7. Canarian group

The Canarian group is the Atlantic threshold: provisioning, quarantine, observatories, convoy
rendezvous, drydock support, fisheries, and migration sorting between Europe, Atlanta, the
Esperanzan belt, and the wider ocean. It functions as a hinge between the European maritime spine
and the global Meridian roll. Its language influence is strongest in route names, shipboard food,
weather terms, dock labor, and emigrant-family vocabulary.

## How territories introduce vocabulary

The Meridian States do not feed vocabulary into Maré by one grand academy list. Words enter by
use, then the Maré seat regularizes them.

- **Service** introduces the words of navy, port police, pilots, cable crews, clerks, stewards,
  dock doctors, surveyors, and interpreters.
- **Migration** introduces household terms, food names, kinship extensions, nursery words,
  neighborhood slang, wedding and funeral vocabulary, and the speech of mixed families.
- **Shipping** introduces cargo, weather, routing, insurance, hull, engine, repair, customs,
  quarantine, and convoy terms.
- **Media** spreads songs, radio formulas, sports speech, dock comedy, serial drama, school
  pronunciation, and prestige urban slang from one territory to another.
- **Education** standardizes spellings, school civics, examinations, teacher vocabulary, legal
  formulas, and the shared names of offices and subjects.
- **Administration** supplies titles, permits, courts, tax categories, police terms, registry
  language, public notices, and federal service ranks.
- **Customary authority** preserves chiefs, councils, elders, lineage offices, temple and church
  roles, land-water rights, feast obligations, and local dispute terms that the federation must
  recognize rather than flatten.

The result is not a grammar revolution. It is a vocabulary ecology. The same invariant spine can
carry Baltic, Saharan, Riverine, and Meridian roots because the constitutional agreement is about
shared structure and archive legibility, not a single world-wide word list.

## Cross-links and continuity

The Meridian States belong to the same family of lore as Neloxia's earlier maritime expansion. The
European files explain the method: modern Neloxia became a sea-chain through layered accessions,
port compacts, corridor provinces, and strategic island relays
([`modern-neloxia-working-lore-model.md`](modern-neloxia-working-lore-model.md)). The expanded-lore
file explains the accession principle: accumulated jurisdiction, petition, compact, and useful law
rather than ethnicity as destiny ([`neloxia-expanded-lore.md`](neloxia-expanded-lore.md)).

Uusatôm is the older prototype for the pattern: a founding-diaspora harbour with its own Neloxian
name, built by traders at distance before modern contiguity could catch up. The Meridian States
repeat that lesson globally. A port can begin as a service settlement, become a diaspora city, grow
its own register, and later receive full constitutional form. For the Uusatôm record, see
[`gazetteer.md`](gazetteer.md) and [`boundaries.md`](boundaries.md).

Maré is therefore the language of the global version of an old Neloxian habit: routes become
institutions; institutions need courts; courts need names; names, households, schools, ships,
media, and customary authorities make a standard.
