## I. The shape of the country

The historical core is a Baltic crescent, hooked rather than smooth. From the map, reading the
arc: the **Prussian anchor** juts south-west (the Kaliningrad core, Kunislinnô); the
**Pomeranian shore belt** bends west along the coast to the Oder gate and makes the anchor the
center of a continuous trading shore rather than an exclave; the **Lithuanian spine** runs north
through the Curonian coast and the Kaunas basin; the **Livonian core** — the islands (Sārô,
Hiumō) and the southeastern Estonian–Latvian interior — faces it across the Gulf; and the
northern arm runs up through the **Vīpôri isthmus** into **Karelia**, the country's large
northern lobe reaching to the White Sea. The eastern land border against Venä is long — the whole eastern flank is a Slavic
contact zone, not a single gateway, which is why the Slavic quarry is primary rather than niche.

**Karelia is directly connected, not overseas.** This is deliberate: it is claimed and held as
part of the broader country, a full constituent region reached overland through the Vīpôri
isthmus — not a department administered from afar. The island chain is ordinary regional
maritime infrastructure, not Karelia's only supply line. Karelia is **not massively populated —
it is simply too cold** — but it is present, worked, and integral: **our own Norway without the
oil.** Sparse in the deep boreal north, dense where settlement makes sense, and the conservative
eastern Finnic (Karelian) strand persists there by demography, cold, and depth of settlement —
the way inland Norwegian dialects stay distinct from Oslo's — not by isolation across a sea.

## II. Scale — a mid-sized European power

Nelôxia is **much larger than the real-world populations of the lands it absorbs would suggest** —
roughly **20 million people** in the pre-expansion core (the shore belt and spine ~15–17M,
Karelia ~3–5M), on a real-world base barely a dozen million. It is Netherlands-sized, not
Latvia-sized: sitting between Sweden (~10M) and Poland (~38M) in the European order. (The full
modern state carries the same premium at scale — ≈76M on a ≈34M real-world base; the working
model has the arithmetic.)

The number is defensible on three axes, all of which the real Baltic lacked:

1. **No Soviet-era stagnation, no post-1945 Kaliningrad collapse-and-refill, no EU-era Baltic
   outmigration.** Nelôxia had a *continuous* trade-and-industry pull for a century and a half
   instead — the profile of the states that grew fast in the industrial era (Netherlands,
   Belgium, Denmark), 400–500/km² on comparable land.
2. **Kunislinnô as a real financial capital** (see below) is a completely different city from
   the real ~450K exclave Kaliningrad — a metro of several million, with the oblast around it
   proportionally larger.
3. **No Baltic peer city.** In the real world, Baltic financial and demographic gravity
   distributes across Stockholm, Copenhagen, Helsinki, Warsaw, and Frankfurt. In Nelôxia none of
   those pull, so the weight concentrates in the country's own cities — amplifying both
   Kunislinnô and the delta crown Tantsika.

## III. The economic character — the European Singapore

The right frame for Nelôxia is **not "Frankfurt meets Vienna" — it is the European Singapore.**
Monaco is a village that moves no goods; Switzerland is a landlocked vault whose banking works
despite its geography. Singapore is different in kind: a **port-based financial and mercantile
state where the geography is the business model** — a chokepoint on a sea lane, financialized by
the throughput, converting neutrality and rule-of-law into stored wealth. **Europe does not have
this**, because after the Hanseatic League decayed no single polity consolidated the Baltic trade
routes — they were carved up between Sweden, the Commonwealth, Prussia, and Russia. Nelôxia is
the counterfactual where one polity *did* consolidate them and stayed neutral and mercantile
through the industrial era.

**Kunislinnô is the financial bulwark of the country** — the one major financial city on the
Baltic, the Baltic's Singapore. It is Frankfurt-scale precisely because it answers to no one
above it. The whole crescent is a break-of-bulk trade machine; the language matches the state
exactly — a polyglot maritime creole with a Latin administrative crust, dozenal counting, a
bureaucracy in Low German, a court in Romance, a market in Slavic, a deck in Scandinavian, and a
Livonian substrate underneath all of it. That is how a Singapore-analog talks.

## IV. The two-ocean state — the Baltic–Arctic–Atlantic corridor

Nelôxia is not a closed Baltic entrepôt dependent on the Danish straits. Because **Karelia gives
the country an Arctic frontage** (the White Sea coast — Korbitô, the Belomorsk/Kemsky reach), the
state is a genuine **intercontinental transit state.** A Baltic → White Sea canal (domestic
infrastructure linking two coasts of one country, the Belomorkanal pattern) → Barents Sea → around
Norway → Atlantic route bypasses the congested North Sea and the foreign chokepoints of the
Danish straits entirely.

This is the piece that makes the state make sense at 20 million rather than 8. **Karelia is not
the remote timber department — it is the northern trunk of the whole business model**, the
northern half of a two-ocean trade route, moving cargo between the Baltic and the Arctic. Every
Karelian city upgrades from outpost to transit node. And the route has a destination: **Atlantic
Sahara** (see [`atlanta-lore.md`](atlanta-lore.md)) — Baltic industrial goods → Arctic → Atlantic
→ Africa, a coherent trade doctrine and a business Rotterdam and Hamburg cannot offer, because no
real European port controls an Arctic-avoidance lane. No real-world country holds both ends of
that transit; Nelôxia does.

## V. The modern posture — sovereignty as the product

**Not in the EU. Not in the Eurozone. Part of the EEC/EEA trade architecture** — it would be
silly not to be; the whole country is a trade machine — but integration stops where sovereignty
begins, for two hard reasons:

**1. The sovereign currency is the keystone.** Nelôxia's role as the Baltic's financial bulwark
depends on monetary sovereignty. A financial entrepôt inside the Eurozone is just another member
bank; one with its own currency, its own rate, and its own lender of last resort is a
*destination* for capital. Kunislinnô is Frankfurt-scale precisely because it is not answerable
to Frankfurt. The currency is also the last institutional strand of the old union with Atlanta,
whose money is shared with or pegged to Nelôxia's (see [`atlanta-lore.md`](atlanta-lore.md)).

**2. Strict border controls — despite, and because of, the polyglot culture.** The polyglot
openness is commercial and cultural, not territorial. A small, rich, strategically placed state
wedged between great powers is permanently vulnerable, and its stability — the very product it
sells — requires knowing exactly who and what crosses. **Open ports, controlled borders.** In the
modern era this is a deliberate and useful instrument, not a contradiction of the country's
character. It is the Singapore settlement.

## VI. The one-paragraph version (for briefs and summaries)

Nelôxia's core is a Baltic crescent of about 20 million — Netherlands-sized, not Latvia-sized —
running from the Oder gate along the Pomeranian shore to the Prussian anchor at Kunislinnô,
up the Lithuanian spine past the Livonian islands, and through the Vīpôri isthmus into a
directly-connected Karelia: sparse because it is cold, but integral — our own Norway without the
oil. It is the
European Singapore, the port-based financial bulwark Europe otherwise lacks, with Kunislinnô as
its Frankfurt-scale financial capital. Karelia's Arctic frontage makes it a two-ocean transit
state on a Baltic–Arctic–Atlantic corridor reaching to Africa. It stays out of the EU and the
euro: a sovereign currency and strict borders are what make a polyglot mercantile entrepôt
durable, and its language — polyglot creole on a Livonian spine with a Latin crust — is the
state's own operating system written down.
