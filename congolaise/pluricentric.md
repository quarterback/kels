# Congolaise and the Three-Center Shape of Nelôxi

Congolaise gives Nelôxi a third center of language life, and in doing so completes the shape the
language has been reaching for: not one supreme registry with provinces, but a **pluricentric**
language of peer seats — the Lusophone pattern carried to its full form, Lisbon and Brasília and
Luanda. Metropolitan Nelôxi, Saharannaise, and Congolaise share one grammatical spine and one
orthographic agreement, and then let their lexicons tell three different histories from three
different worlds.

The shared grammar is the constitutional layer, and it does more work with each seat that joins.
It kept sentence structure legible when Saharannaise flipped the domestic root-stock to Arabic and
Berber; it keeps it legible again as Congolaise draws its core from Pulaar, Soninke-Mande, and
Wolof — a third stock, from a third language family, unrelated to the Baltic quarries and unrelated
to the Afro-Asiatic base of the coast. A root from any of these can enter, take Nelôxi morphology,
decline through Nelôxi grammar, and function inside the same system as a mainland root. Three
unrelated families declining their nouns by one dead coastal-Finnic grammar: that is not
inheritance, it is infrastructure, and the third seat is the proof that the infrastructure scales.

Congolaise also raises the register of the demonstration. Saharannaise proved the thesis with a
single substrate under a single superstrate — a clean two-source reroot. The Senegal-River valley
is a harder and richer case: three living tongues coexisting as peers, each with its own domains of
authority. Congolaise does not flatten that into one substrate; it keeps the three and governs
their meeting with a doctrine of its own — the **Confluence Principle** — assigning each tributary
its territory (Soninke-Mande the classical trade-and-statecraft register, Pulaar the pastoral and
domestic core, Wolof the coastal and urban edge) and letting them converge on the shared spine the
way the tributaries converge into the river the state is named for. Where Saharannaise reroots,
Congolaise **fuses** — and the grammar carries a whole creole, not just a swapped word-list.

The three seats produce the same asymmetric intelligibility, now triangulated. Formal documents,
law, and the Romance learned crust stay readable across all three, because they ride the shared
spine. The market drifts one root at a time; the cattle-camp, the floodplain field, the griot's
recitation, and the family compound go fully local, three different ways at once. A speaker trained
in any coastal standard moves through metropolitan writing with ease; a mainlander arriving in Ndar,
as in Daxla, needs exposure and local guidance. Intelligibility runs toward the trunk, and the
trunk in turn receives what the branches send back: the Saharan *gāfila* rode up the routes to the
mainland ports, and now the riverine *kora*, *jembe*, *jula*, and *terānga* ride up behind it —
prestige loans that enter another seat's canon only when that seat chooses to merge them.

Nelôxi becomes fully pluricentric through this third seat. The coursebook standard, Saharannaise,
and Congolaise share one grammatical agreement, then let their dictionaries carry a mainland
archive, an Atlantic archive, and a Riverine archive — each capable of producing canon, prestige,
slang, and memory from its own world, none subordinate to another, all recognizably one language.
