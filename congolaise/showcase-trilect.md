# Showcase — one scene, three standards

The sturdiness thesis shown at full stretch: the same sentence in **Metropolitan Nelôxi**,
**Saharannaise**, and **Congolaise**. The structure is identical in all three — same word order,
same copula *um*, same negation *äb*, same dozenal numbers, same object case — so the skeleton is
always transparent. What changes is the content roots, and *how many* change is exactly what tier
the line sits in. That is the whole thesis on a page: **one spine, three lexicons; the grammar
never drifts, the vocabulary does all the diverging** — now across three worlds, not two.

Read the Metropolitan line as the mainland clerk writes it, the Saharannaise as the Daxla
dockworker says it, the Congolaise as the Ndar market-woman says it.

---

## Level 1 — formal & archival (all three read it)

The shared Romance learned crust carries the formal register in every standard, so an
administrative line is transparent across the whole language.

> **Metr.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Sah.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Cong.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> *province has three regions* — "The province has three regions."

Identical in all three. This is Level 1: shared frame, shared Romance content. The spine and the
learned crust are the common ground of the entire pluricentric language.

## Level 2 — one root swapped (a mainlander manages)

> **Metr.**  Markôt um sūr.
> **Sah.**  Sūg um sūr.
> **Cong.**  Lumo um sūr.
> *market is big* — "The market is big."

The frame *um sūr* is shared and instant; only the word for "market" diverges — mainland
**markôt**, Saharan **sūg**, riverine **lumo** (Wol.). Three markets, one skeleton. A mainlander
with a little exposure recovers any of them; each coastal speaker, schooled in the standard, reads
the mainland line with no trouble. The asymmetry runs the same one way in both directions from the
trunk.

## Level 3 — the reroot bites (a mainlander is lost)

> **Metr.**  Nahtô um kylm.
> **Sah.**  Līl um barād.
> **Cong.**  Jemma um ɓūɓ.
> *night is cold* — "The night is cold."

Same skeleton, but now the content has gone local in each seat: "night" is mainland **nahtô**,
Saharan **līl** (Hass.), riverine **jemma** (Pul.); "cold" is **kylm**, **barād** (the desert cold
of winter nights), **ɓūɓ** (Pul. *ɓuuɓ-*, cool). Two swaps and the copula is the only thing a
mainlander still holds — and the Congolaise line carries the **ɓ scar** that marks its stratum at a
glance. This is Level 3: the domestic layer fully rerooted, near-zero intelligibility outward,
three different ways.

## The hybrid seam — the same machine in three hands

> **Sah.**  Žəmlôr tulē.  *(camel-driver comes)*
> **Cong.**  Gaynôr tulē.  *(herder comes)*
> *— "The cameleer / the herder comes."*

The verb *tulē* "comes" is coursebook canon in both. The agent is built the same way in each seat —
a local root plus the **exact mainland suffix -ôr**: Saharan *žəml* → **žəmlôr**, Pulaar *gayn* →
**gaynôr**. The machine does not care which sea, or which river, the root came from. That is the
thesis in one suffix.

## Back-flow — the loan travels up the trunk

> **both trunk & coast:**  Jeli tulē.
> *griot comes* — "The griot comes."

"Griot" is a gap in Metropolitan and in Saharannaise alike — neither Baltic nor Saharan culture has
the hereditary lineage-keeper the river valley does. As Sotoran music and scholarship travel the
trade routes, **jeli** (and *kora*, *jembe*, *terānga*) ride up into the metropolitan and Atlantic
lexicons — first as the only available term, then as a prestige loan. Flagged for future mainland
and Saharan batches; each becomes another standard's canon only by that seat's merge, never
automatically. The confluence feeds the trunk, exactly as the Saharan *gāfila* once did.
