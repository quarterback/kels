# Congolaise Reader — *Gan ja Lumo* (The Guest and the Market)

*A spoken dialogue in Congolaise: the phrasebook lines connected into real back-and-forth speech.
Two scenes — a traveler arriving at a river village in **Futa** and taken in under *terānga*, then
haggling at the **lumo** in **Ndar**. Built by `composition.md`; every frame-word is coursebook
canon, every content root archived Congolaise (`phrasebook.md`, lexicons R/P/G). It is the
conversational counterpart of the mainland `../reader/dialogue-haggling.md` and the narrative
`reader-flood.md`. The point: the close-contact register **works as dialogue** — the greeting gates
everything, the spine carries the exchange, the Congolaise roots do the world. Speakers: **G** the
guest (a traveler up from the coast), **B** an elder host (*bāba*), **D** a market-woman.*

---

## Scene 1 · The welcome (a *wuro* in Futa)

> **B.** Jam?
> *peace?* — "Well?"
>
> **G.** Jam. Sä, jam?
> *peace. you, peace?* — "Well. And you?"
>
> **B.** Jam kôg. Sä tulē kus-lt?
> *peace all. you come where-from?* — "All's well. Where are you from?"
>
> **G.** Mä tulē Ndar-lt. Mä sōv sira Futa-lô.
> *I come Ndar-from. I want road Futa-to.* — "I've come from Ndar. I'm looking for the road up to Futa."
>
> **B.** Tulē, gan! Velkô. Saistā sīn.
> *come, guest! welcome. stay here.* — "Come in, guest! Welcome. Sit, rest."
>
> **G.** Jārāma, bāba.
> *thanks, father.* — "Thank you, elder."
>
> **B.** Tulē, menjā. Debo kuinai cēb ja jën.
> *come, eat. woman cooked rice and fish.* — "Come and eat. My wife has cooked *ceeb u jën*."
>
> **G.** Cēb um jôvā sūr. Jārāma.
> *rice is good big. thanks.* — "The rice is very good. Thank you."
>
> **B.** Terānga! Gan um giɗo.
> *hospitality! guest is friend.* — "It's *terānga* — a guest is a friend."
>
> **G.** Mä pidā lǟdô kikīɗe-al.
> *I must go evening-at.* — "I ought to leave by evening."
>
> **B.** Nē — saistā. Jemma tulē. Lǟdô subaka-al.
> *no — stay. night comes. go morning-at.* — "No — stay. Night's coming. Go in the morning."
>
> **G.** Jā, jārāma. Sä um giɗo sūr.
> *yes, thanks. you are friend big.* — "Alright — thank you. You're a good friend."

## Scene 2 · The bargain (the *lumo* at Ndar)

> **G.** Jam?
> *peace?* — "Hello?"
>
> **D.** Jam, jam. Mis sä sōv?
> *peace, peace. what you want?* — "Hello, hello. What do you need?"
>
> **G.** Gerte. Mis prēu?
> *groundnuts. what price?* — "Groundnuts. How much?"
>
> **D.** Düna drobô.
> *twelve coins.* — "Twelve."
>
> **G.** Nē! Sūr mult. Jay petīt, bidô.
> *no! big much. sell small, please.* — "No! That's too much. Come down a bit, please."
>
> **D.** Sä faam — gerte um jôvā.
> *you understand — groundnuts are good.* — "But you see — these are good groundnuts."
>
> **G.** Jā, jā. Agā mä sōv tri.
> *yes, yes. but I want three.* — "Yes, yes — but I'll give three."
>
> **D.** *(yimi)* Sä um giɗo! Jā — gerte um sä-lô.
> *(she laughs) you are friend! yes — groundnuts are you-to.* — "Ha, you're a friend! Fine — they're yours."
>
> **G.** Jārāma! Annõ drobô?
> *thanks! give change?* — "Thank you! My change?"
>
> **D.** Sīn. Lǟdô jam-k, giɗo.
> *here. go with-peace, friend.* — "Here. Go in peace, friend."
>
> **G.** Jam-k!
> *with-peace!* — "Peace!"

## What it demonstrates (the register, in motion)

- **The greeting gates everything.** Both scenes open with *Jam?* and cannot proceed without the
  return. The bargain does not start until peace is exchanged — the phrasebook's first rule, shown
  live. Skipping it would read as hostile.
- **The spine carries the conversation.** Questions (*Mis prēu?* "what price?", *Sä tulē kus-lt?*
  "where from?"), negation (*Nē!*, *äb*), modality (*pidā* "must"), the number words (*tri*, *düna*),
  the pronouns (*mä, sä*), the goal and comitative cases (*Futa-lô*, *sä-lô*, *jam-k*) — all
  coursebook canon, identical to what a mainlander uses. A Metropolitan speaker can *follow the shape*
  of both scenes even while the content stays local.
- **The content is Congolaise, by domain.** Hospitality and food from the Pulaar-Wolof core
  (*gan, terānga, cēb, jën, giɗo, wuro, jārāma*), the market from the Wolof edge (*lumo, gerte, jay,
  prēu*), each root from the tributary the Confluence Principle assigns it.
- **The culture is in the moves, not glossed at you.** *Bāba* "father" as respectful address to an
  elder; the host overriding the guest's polite exit (*Nē — saistā*) because turning out a traveler
  at nightfall is unthinkable; the haggle closing not on a number but on *Sä um giɗo!* — the deal
  sealed by friendship, not arithmetic. This is *terānga* as behavior.
- **No calque, no wrong well.** Hospitality is *terānga*, not a "guest-kindness" compound; the
  market is *lumo*, not the mainland *markôt*. The reroot holds through a whole conversation.
