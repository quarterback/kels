# Congolaise Reader — *Māyo ja Wālo* (The River and the Floodplain)

*A river narrative in Congolaise: the first connected Congolaise prose beyond the showcase lines.
Built strictly by `composition.md` — Metropolitan grammar and function words carrying Niger-Congo
content roots (lexicons R, P, G), each root drawn from the tributary that owns its domain
(`CONFLUENCE-PRINCIPLE.md` §1). Every frame-word is coursebook canon; every content word is archived
Congolaise. Past-tense narration (§120 two classes, shared). It is the deliberate counterpart of the
Saharan `../saharannaise/reader-caravan.md`: where the desert scene turns on drought and a sandstorm,
the river scene turns on the flood and the griot — the same skeleton, the opposite ecology. Tier:
mostly Level 3 (a mainlander is lost in the nouns, at home in the structure), closing on a Level-1
turn.*

---

## The tale

> **1.** Ndungu tulī, ja toɓo tulī māyo-lô.
> *The rainy season came, and the rain came to the river.*
>
> **2.** Māyo ūli sūr, ja waṃe kohatai wālôn.
> *The river was high, and the flood reached the floodplain.*
>
> **3.** Gorko ja debo lǟdi ngesa-lô, ja awi māron.
> *The man and the woman went to the field, and sowed rice.*
>
> **4.** Wālo ūli felīç, ja ngesa donai gawrin.
> *The floodplain was rich, and the field gave grain.*
>
> **5.** Gaynôr kandai nāyin huɗo-lô, ja nagge donai kosamôn.
> *The herder brought the herd to the grass, and the cattle gave milk.*
>
> **6.** Ɓiɗo lǟdi māyo-lô, ja napi jënôn gāl-al.
> *The child went to the river, and caught fish from the pirogue.*
>
> **7.** Kikīɗe tulī, ja wuro ūli jam-k.
> *Evening came, and the village was at peace (with-peace).*
>
> **8.** Jeli tulī wuro-lô, ja yimi kora-k.
> *The griot came to the village, and sang with the kora.*
>
> **9.** Debo kuinai māron, ja wuro menjai jënôn ja gawrin.
> *The woman cooked rice, and the village ate fish and grain.*
>
> **10.** Jemma tulī, ja ɓūɓ tulī māyo-lt. Nagge saistai wuro-al.
> *Night came, and the cold came off the river. The cattle stood in the village.*
>
> **11.** Tôl jeli dīsi: "Terānga um sūr. Gan pidā menjā ja saistā."
> *Then the griot said: "Hospitality is great. The guest must eat and stay."*

## What it demonstrates (the thesis, in prose)

- **The skeleton never drifts.** SVO throughout; copula *um / ūli*; past *-i* (weak: *kohatai,
  donai, awi, napi, kandai, saistai, kuinai, menjai*; strong *tulī*; monosyllabic *dīsi*); modal
  *pidā*; the case system doing all the role-marking. A mainlander parses every sentence's
  *structure* effortlessly — exactly as with the Saharan caravan.
- **The Niger-Congo roots decline like any noun.** *wālôn* (floodplain + object *-n*), *māron /
  gawrin / jënôn / kosamôn / nāyin* (objects), *māyo-lô* (river + goal "to"), *māyo-lt* (river +
  ablative "off/from"), *huɗo-lô* (grass + goal), *gāl-al / wuro-al* (adessive "on/in"), *kora-k /
  jam-k* (comitative "with"). The grammar does not know the roots came from across a continent.
- **Composition moves, not new words:** *jam-k* "with-peace = peaceful" and *kora-k* "with the kora"
  (the comitative doing the work, no coined adjective) — the same move as the caravan's *aṭx-k*
  "with-thirst = thirsty."
- **Three tributaries, one paragraph.** Pulaar carries the domestic core (*māyo, ngesa, nagge, huɗo,
  gorko, debo, ɓiɗo, kosam, wuro, jemma, ɓūɓ, jam*); Wolof carries the coast and market (*gāl, jën,
  wālo, gan*); Soninke-Mande carries the cultural-classical (*jeli, kora* by association, *terānga*
  from the hospitality edge). Each root sits in the domain the Confluence Principle assigns it.
- **The reroot bites (Level 3), and the compound ban held.** The griot is *jeli*, not a "song-teller";
  the floodplain is *wālo*, not a "wet-field." No Baltic calque anywhere. The passage is opaque to an
  untrained mainlander and fluent to an Ndar farmer.
- **The trilect echo.** Sentence 10 is deliberately the caravan's night-sentence rebuilt: *Jemma
  tulī, ja ɓūɓ tulī māyo-lt* mirrors the Saharan *Līl tulī, ja barād tulī erg-lt* — same frame,
  same past, the cold coming off the river instead of off the sand. One skeleton, two worlds.

## The gap-list — five headwords this text surfaced

Per `composition.md` §6, a corpus text's most valuable output is the gaps it exposes. Writing *Māyo
ja Wālo* required five roots the batches did not yet hold; the Kēļs Penc coins them here, from the
owning tributary, through the fused phonology (`CONFLUENCE-PRINCIPLE.md` §2). They are now Congolaise
canon, extending their domains.

| Headword | PoS | Tributary · source | Gloss | Extends | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **toɓo** | n. | [Pul.] *toɓo* "rain" | rain (the falling rain, the event) | Batch P (seasons) | **ɓ scar** — the rain itself wears the stratum fingerprint. |
| **kikīɗe** | n. | [Pul.] *kikiiɗe* "evening" | evening, the late day | Batch P (day) | *ii→ī*, **ɗ scar.** |
| **subaka** | n. | [Pul.] *subaka* "morning" | morning, the early day | Batch P (day) | Paired with *kikīɗe*; the two ends of the day. |
| **aw** | v./root | [Pul.] *awde* "to sow" | to sow, to plant (seed) | Batch R (fields) | Root bare; past *awi*. |
| **yim** | v./root | [Pul.] *yimde* "to sing" | to sing, to recite (as the griot does) | Batch G (griot) | Root bare; past *yimi*. The griot's own verb. |
