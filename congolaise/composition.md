# Congolaise — Writing Connected Prose (the composition guide)

*How to write Congolaise, not just list its words. The Atlantic counterpart is
`../saharannaise/composition.md`; the metrolect's original is `../grammar/14-composition.md`. The
rule that governs everything here is the sturdiness thesis (`congolaise.md`) under the Confluence
Principle (`CONFLUENCE-PRINCIPLE.md`): **the spine does the grammar, the three tributaries do the
content.** You build a Congolaise sentence exactly as a Metropolitan one — same skeleton, same
function words — and swap in the Niger-Congo root, drawn from whichever tributary owns the domain.
If a mainlander can parse the structure of your sentence while being lost in its nouns, you have
written good Congolaise.*

---

## 1 · The two halves of every sentence

- **The spine is shared and invariant** (`../saharannaise/COMMON-REGISTER.md` §1). Word order
  **SVO**; negation pre-verbal **äb**; copula **um / ūli**; past **-i** (two classes, §120); modals
  **pidā** "must"; the case endings (object **-n/-ôn**, adessive **-al** "at/on", ablative **-lt**
  "from/off", goal **-lô** "to/toward", comitative **-k** "with"); the derivation suffixes
  (**-ôr, -in, -mus, -stā, -ldā, -tā, -mi**); the dozenal numbers. **You do not touch any of it.**
- **The content is rerooted, by domain.** For the river and the field, the cattle and the kin, the
  gold and the griot — reach for the **Niger-Congo** root (lexicons R, P, G), and reach into the
  **tributary that owns that domain** (the Confluence Principle §1): Soninke-Mande for trade and
  gold and rule, Pulaar for the pastoral-domestic core, Wolof for the coast and market and city.
  The function words stay Metropolitan (*um, äb, ja, tôl, tulē, pidā, -al, -lt, -lô, -n*).

So a sentence is: **[Metropolitan grammar + function words] + [Niger-Congo content roots, by
domain].**

> *Waṃe kohatai wālôn.* — "The flood reached the floodplain."
> The frame (*kohatai* "reached," *-ôn* object) is coursebook; the content (*waṃe*, *wālo*) is
> Congolaise, both from the river domain [R]. A mainlander reads the skeleton and is lost in the
> nouns — the Level-3 target.

## 2 · The reroot bites — name the reality, and from the right well

Two rules bite here, one shared and one unique to Congolaise:

- **The compound ban** (shared, `../saharannaise/saharannaise.md` §1). Never build a Baltic calque
  for a Sahelian reality. A griot is **jeli**, not a "song-teller"; flood-recession land is
  **wālo**, not a "wet-field." Name the reality; do not translate it.
- **The right-well rule** (Congolaise's own, `CONFLUENCE-PRINCIPLE.md` §1). Do not reach across the
  domain line. In a cattle-camp you want the Pulaar root (*nagge*, *huɗo*); in a gold-caravan the
  Soninke-Mande one (*sanu*, *jula*, *sira*); at the market and the port the Wolof one (*lumo*,
  *gāl*, *jën*). Reaching for a Wolof market-word in a pasture scene is as much an error as reaching
  for a Baltic one.

- ✅ *Gaynôr kandai nāyin huɗo-lô* "the herder brought the herd to the grass" (pasture → Pulaar)
- ✅ *Jula lǟdi sira-läbi* "the trader went along the road" (trade → Soninke-Mande)
- ❌ *lumo-nagge* "market-cattle" for a herd at pasture — wrong well; the market word does not belong
  in the pasture.

## 3 · Hybrid morphology — the seam, and the discipline

Niger-Congo root + mainland suffix is fully productive (`CONFLUENCE-PRINCIPLE.md` §3). Build agents
and derived forms on any tributary's root with the ordinary coursebook machinery — **but borrow no
tributary's own agent morpheme.** The root is taken bare; the mainland **-ôr** does the work:

- agent **-ôr**: *dem* → **demôr** "farmer" · *nap* → **napôr** "fisher" · *gayn* → **gaynôr**
  "herder" · *sanu* → **sanuôr** "gold-panner" · *kolon* → **kolonôr** "well-keeper" · *dugu* →
  **duguôr** "townsman."
- The refused morphemes — Pulaar *-oowo/-aako*, Wolof *-kat*, Mande *-tigi* — never enter. *demôr*,
  not *demoowo*; *duguôr*, not *dugutigi*. Root borrowed, morpheme refused: that *is* the thesis.

## 4 · Know your tier — write to the intelligibility you intend

Every sentence sits at a transparency tier (`congolaise.md` §2); good writing is deliberate about
which:

- **Level 1 (all three standards read it)** — the Romance learned crust and the shared spine. Law,
  the decree, the manifest. *Provinsô agā tri rīgô* "the province has three regions." A mainland
  clerk, a Daxla clerk, and an Ndar clerk all read it.
- **Level 2 (a mainlander manages)** — one content root swapped: *Lumo um sūr* "the market is big"
  (*markôt → lumo*). The frame carries it.
- **Level 3 (a mainlander is lost)** — the domestic core fully rerooted: *Jemma um ɓūɓ* "the night
  is cold." Home speech, the floodplain and the cattle-camp and the griot's recitation. Write here
  when the scene is domestic and riverine.

## 5 · A worked paragraph — the flood reaches the field

Every content root below is archived Congolaise (lexicons R, P, G); every frame-word is coursebook.

1. *Ndungu tulī, ja māyo ūli sūr.* — "The rainy season came, and the river was high." *(ndungu,
   māyo Congolaise; the copula and past coursebook.)*
2. *Waṃe kohatai wālôn, ja gorko awi māron.* — "The flood reached the floodplain, and the man sowed
   rice." *(waṃe, wālo, gorko, māro Congolaise; kohatai, awi, object -ôn/-n coursebook.)*
3. *Gaynôr kandai nāyin huɗo-lô.* — "The herder brought the herd to the grass." *(gaynôr, nāyi,
   huɗo Congolaise; the verb, the goal -lô coursebook.)*
4. *Kikīɗe-al, jeli yimi kora-k.* — "At evening, the griot sang with the kora." *(kikīɗe, jeli, kora
   Congolaise; the adessive -al and comitative -k coursebook.)*

**Run together:**

> *Ndungu tulī, ja māyo ūli sūr. Waṃe kohatai wālôn, ja gorko awi māron. Gaynôr kandai nāyin
> huɗo-lô. Kikīɗe-al, jeli yimi kora-k.*

The skeleton never drifts — SVO, the copula, the cases, the past **-i** — and the roots of three
tributaries decline through it like any noun. That is a paragraph of Congolaise: mainland grammar,
the river world, three tongues converged.

## 6 · When a word is missing — flag it for the Penc

If you need a domestic word Congolaise does not yet have, **do not** calque it from Baltic roots and
**do not** borrow a Baltic word. Flag it as a gap for the **Kēļs Penc** — the root is coined by the
Forum from the tributary that owns the domain (§1), exactly as the coursebook coins Metropolitan
words and the Dīwān coins Saharan ones. Shared *grammar* words are never a Congolaise matter; those
are joint, ruled by the Kolēgi with the Dīwān and the Penc. The gap-list of a corpus text is its
most valuable output — see the reader below, whose gaps became five new headwords.
