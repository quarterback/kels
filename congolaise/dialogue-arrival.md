# Congolaise Reader — *Arriving in Ndar* (a visitor's day, in dialogue)

*Realistic modern dialogue in Congolaise — the phrasebook (`phrasebook.md`) run as connected speech
across a visitor's first hours on the **Atlantic-Africa** coast: the taxi in from the terminal,
checking into a hotel, and a café. The register is contemporary travel, not folklore: the modern
infrastructure words are the **shared crust** (transparent coast-wide — *taksô, otelô, pāsportô,
wifī, kambôr*), the human exchange is **Congolaise**, and the greeting gates each one. Every
frame-word is coursebook canon; every content root is archived Congolaise. This is the Congolaise
(Soninka & Toro) leg of the coast-wide `../reader/phrasebook.md`.*

---

## Scene 1 · The taxi (terminal → hotel)

> **Visitor.** Jam?
> *— "Hello?"*
>
> **Driver.** Jam, jam. Kus-lô?
> *peace, peace. where-to?* — "Hello. Where to?"
>
> **Visitor.** Otelô Māyo-lô. Mis prēu?
> *hotel Māyo-to. what price?* — "To the Hotel Māyo. How much?"
>
> **Driver.** Düna.
> *twelve.* — "Twelve." *(dozenal — the coast's round number)*
>
> **Visitor.** Nē — sūr mult. Petīt, bidô.
> *no — big much. small, please.* — "No — too much. A bit less, please."
>
> **Driver.** *(laughs)* Jā, jā. Tulē.
> *yes, yes. come.* — "Alright, alright. Get in."
>
> **Visitor.** Jārāma. Otelô kaug?
> *thanks. hotel far?* — "Thanks. Is the hotel far?"
>
> **Driver.** Nē, prop. Düna minutô.
> *no, near. twelve minutes.* — "No, close. About twelve minutes." *(minutô, crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Sīn, bidô. Püsām.
> *here, please. stop.* — "Here, please. Stop."

## Scene 2 · The hotel desk

> **Clerk.** Jam? Velkô.
> *peace? welcome.* — "Hello, welcome."
>
> **Visitor.** Jam. Mä sōv kambôr — tri pǟvô.
> *peace. I want room — three days.* — "Hello. I'd like a room, for three nights."
>
> **Clerk.** Jā. Pāsportô, bidô.
> *yes. passport, please.* — "Certainly. Your passport, please." *(pāsportô, crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Sīn. Wifī um?
> *here. wi-fi is?* — "Here you go. Is there wi-fi?"
>
> **Clerk.** Jā, wifī um jôvā. Sīn um vôtī.
> *yes, wi-fi is good. here is key.* — "Yes, good wi-fi. Here's your key." *(wifī, vôtī crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Jārāma. Ndiyam um jôvā?
> *thanks. water is good?* — "Thank you. Is the tap water safe?"
>
> **Clerk.** Nē — sōv ndiyam-butelô. Farmasī um prop, lumo-al.
> *no — want water-bottle. pharmacy is near, market-at.* — "No — get bottled water. There's a
> pharmacy nearby, by the market." *(butelô, farmasī crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Jārāma sūr.
> *thanks big.* — "Thanks very much."

## Scene 3 · The café

> **Waiter.** Jam? Mis sä sōv?
> *peace? what you want?* — "Hello. What can I get you?"
>
> **Visitor.** Jam. Kafē, ja... mis um jôvā avuī?
> *peace. coffee, and… what is good today?* — "Hello. A coffee, and — what's good today?"
>
> **Waiter.** Cēb ja jën. Um jôvā sūr.
> *rice and fish. is good big.* — "The *ceebu jën*. It's very good."
>
> **Visitor.** Jā, bidô. Mä äb menjā lxam — jën, jôvā?
> *yes, please. I not eat meat — fish, good?* — "Yes, please. I don't eat meat — just fish, OK?"
> *(lxam "meat" — a coast-shared food word from the Saharan side)*
>
> **Waiter.** Jôvā. Jën.
> *good. fish.* — "No problem. Fish it is."
>
> *— later —*
>
> **Visitor.** Um jôvā sūr. Jārāma. Prēu, bidô?
> *is good big. thanks. price, please?* — "That was excellent. Thank you. The bill, please?"
>
> **Waiter.** Sīn. Rā um kartô jôvā.
> *here. money — card is good.* — "Here. Card is fine." *(kartô, crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Jārāma. Lǟdô jam-k.
> *thanks. go with-peace.* — "Thank you. Take care."
>
> **Waiter.** Jam wuro-lô.
> *peace to-household.* — "Regards to your family."

## What it demonstrates (a modern register, working)

- **Two layers, cleanly split.** The *infrastructure* is the shared crust, transparent to any Nelôxi
  speaker and identical on the Atlanta / Adrāra side of the coast: *taksô, otelô, pāsportô, wifī,
  kambôr, vôtī, farmasī, kartô, minutô, butelô*. The *human* layer — greeting, thanks, taste,
  courtesy — is Congolaise: *jam, jārāma, cēb, jën, ndiyam, jôvā, jam-k*. A visitor learns the crust
  once for the whole coast, the Congolaise for the warmth.
- **The greeting still gates everything** — taxi, desk, and café each open with *Jam?* and its
  return. It is not folklore; it is how a transaction politely begins here.
- **The spine carries the modern conversation.** Questions (*Mis prēu?*, *Kus-lô?*, *Wifī um?*),
  negation (*Nē*, *äb*), the cases (*otelô-lô* "to the hotel", *lumo-al* "at the market", *jam-k* "in
  peace"), the dozenal price (*düna* "twelve") — all coursebook grammar, the same a mainlander uses.
- **The coast is one economy.** *lxam* "meat" is borrowed in from the Saharan (Atlanta) side — a
  shared food word travelling down the coast — showing Atlantic Africa as one connected world.
- **Contemporary, not quaint.** Card payment, bottled water, wi-fi, a pharmacy by the market — a real
  modern visit — closed with the ordinary local sign-off *Lǟdô jam-k* / *Jam wuro-lô*.
