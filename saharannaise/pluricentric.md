# Saharannaise and the Pluricentric Shape of Nelôxi

Saharannaise gives Nelôxi a second center of language life. Its importance comes from the way it lets a shared grammar carry separate histories, institutions, landscapes, and vocabularies across distance. Metropolitan Nelôxi and Saharannaise remain connected through a common grammatical spine, shared orthographic agreement, and parallel standards of use. Their lexicons grow from different social worlds.

The shared grammar acts like a constitutional layer. It keeps sentence structure, cases, negation, derivation, number, and archive spelling legible across both standards. A root from Hassaniya Arabic or Zenaga Berber can enter Saharannaise, take Nelôxi morphology, decline through Nelôxi grammar, and function inside the same system as a mainland root. This makes the grammar more than inheritance. It becomes infrastructure.

Saharannaise also gives the language a working model for independent lexical evolution. Atlanta has its own courts, ports, schools, newspapers, markets, religious life, maritime labor, and interior frontier. Those institutions generate vocabulary from local conditions. The result is a standard whose domestic and ecological words belong to the Atlantic Sahara, even as its syntax remains recognizably Nelôxian.

The relationship between the two standards creates asymmetric intelligibility. Formal documents, legal notices, shipping records, and administrative formulas remain highly readable across the system because they rely on the shared spine. Street speech, market speech, family language, weather terms, animal vocabulary, and interior place names carry deeper Saharan roots. A trained Saharannaise speaker may move through metropolitan writing with ease, while a mainland speaker arriving in Atlanta needs exposure, practice, and local guidance.

This makes Saharannaise a language of identity, governance, and daily use. Its academy, the Kēļs Dīwān, gives local authority to the Atlantic standard. Its vocabulary records wells, winds, fish works, ports, caravans, markets, and households. Its forms can travel back into Metropolitan Nelôxi through sailors, clerks, pilots, soldiers, radio, schooling, and trade.

Nelôxi becomes pluricentric through use. The coursebook standard and Saharannaise share one grammatical agreement, then let their dictionaries tell different histories. The result is a language with a mainland archive and an Atlantic archive, each capable of producing canon, prestige, slang, and memory from its own world.
