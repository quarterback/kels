# Saharannaise Lexicon — Domain E · Batch 5
### Caravan · Faith · The Guest-Law

The registers that govern movement and obligation in the Atlantic Sahara: the caravan and the
desert crossing; religious life; and *ḍiyāfa*, the guest-law — the sacred hospitality that makes
travel across an empty land survivable, and which is the deep root of Atlanta's whole character as
the safe place. Sourced **Hassaniya Arabic (Hass.)** and **Zenaga Berber (Zen.)**, processed
through `COMMON-REGISTER.md` (qaf→g, emphatics ṭ ṣ ḍ ẓ as scars, ʿayn kept, š/x per §47). Real
words borrowed whole; no Baltic calques.

---

| **žəml** | n. | Hass. *žml* "camel" | camel *(canonized in lexicon B — cross-listed here, not re-counted)* | The vessel of the sand-sea; cf. **žəmlôr** who drives it and **žəmlān** the racing camel (both lexicon B). |

## The guest-law — ḍiyāfa

| Headword | PoS | Source root | Gloss | Doublet / note |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **ḍiyāfa** | n. | Hass. *ḍiyāfa* | the guest-law; sacred hospitality — the obligation to shelter, feed, and protect a traveler | Emphatic ḍ scar. THE root institution: why the Atlantic Sahara became the safe place. Shelter is not charity, it is law. |
| **ḍīf** | n. | Hass. *ḍīf* "guest" | the guest; the one owed protection | The guest is sacred for three days by right, no questions asked. |
| **mūl əddār** | n.phr. | Hass. *mūl* "owner" + *dār* "house" | the host; the master of the house who owes the guest-duty | The obligation runs to him personally. |
| **əl-ḥagg** | n. | Hass. *ḥaqq* (qaf→g) | the right, the due; what is owed by law and honor | The guest has ḥagg on the host; a broken due is a broken name (cf. mainland *nimpundô*). |
| **atāy** | n. | Hass. *atāy* | tea; the three-glass ritual of welcome *(cross-ref lexicon C — cross-listed, not re-counted)* | The physical act of ḍiyāfa — you are not a guest until the tea is poured. |
| **žār** | n. | Hass. *jār* "neighbor" | the neighbor; the one whose nearness creates duty | j→ž. Neighborliness as obligation, not sentiment. |

**19 new headwords** (plus **žəml**, canonized in `lexicon-B-*`, and **atāy**, canonized in
`lexicon-C-*` — both cross-listed here, not re-counted). The
through-line: in a land where a lost traveler dies, **hospitality is law and faith is order** —
ḍiyāfa and əddīn together make the desert survivable and made Atlanta the safe place capital came
to rest. The guest-law (*ḍiyāfa*, *ḥagg*) rhymes with the mainland honor-complex (*nimpundô*,
*prisęga*): both cultures make the keeping of obligation the load-bearing virtue — the Baltic
through the name and the contract, the Sahara through the guest and the due. Two shores, one moral
spine.

## Cross-listing & resonance

- **atāy** "tea; the three-glass welcome" is canonized in `lexicon-C-food-kin-market-day.md`
  (Batch 2) and cross-listed here for its role in the guest-law: it is the *act* of ḍiyāfa, the
  ritual by which shelter is enacted, not merely offered.
- **žəml** "camel" is canonized in `lexicon-B-*` alongside **žəmlôr** "cameleer" (a proof-word for
  the register's productive morphology, Saharan root + mainland *-ôr*) and **žəmlān** "racing
  camel" — cross-listed here as the vessel of the crossing.
- **əl-ḥagg** (the due) and **ḍiyāfa** (the guest-law) are the Saharan face of the same moral load
  the mainland carries in *nimpundô* (name-honor) and *prisęga* (the sworn oath): obligation kept
  is the culture's load-bearing virtue on both shores.
