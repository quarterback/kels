# Saharannaise — Writing Connected Prose (the composition guide)

*How to write Saharannaise, not just list its words. The metrolect's writer's guide is
`grammar/14-composition.md`; this is its Atlantic counterpart. The rule that governs everything here
is the sturdiness thesis (`saharannaise.md`): **the spine does the grammar, the reroot does the
content.** You build a Saharannaise sentence exactly as you build a Metropolitan one — same skeleton,
same function words — and swap in the Saharan roots for the lived, domestic, ecological layer. If a
mainlander can parse the *structure* of your sentence while being lost in its *content*, you have
written good Saharannaise.*

---

## 1 · The two halves of every sentence

- **The spine is shared and invariant** (`COMMON-REGISTER.md` §1). Word order **SVO**; negation
  pre-verbal **äb**; copula **um / ūli**; past **-i** (two classes, §120); modals **pidā** "must" /
  **vôī** "may"; the case endings, the derivation suffixes (**-ôr, -in, -mus, -stā, -ldā, -tā, -mi**),
  the dozenal numbers. **You do not touch any of it.** Write the skeleton as the coursebook does.
- **The content is rerooted.** For **terrain, water, weather, livestock, food, kin, the market, the
  day** — the whole domestic layer — reach for the **Saharan** root (lexicons B, C), never a Baltic
  one. The function words stay Metropolitan (*um, äb, ja, agā, ken, tulē, elkü, pidā, sīn, tôl*); the
  *content* words go Hassaniya or Zenaga.

So a sentence is: **[Metropolitan grammar + function words] + [Saharan content roots].**

> *Gāfila lǟdi erg-läbi.* — "The caravan went through the sand-sea."
> The frame (*lǟdi* "went," *läbi* "through") is coursebook; the content (*gāfila*, *erg*) is Saharan.
> A mainlander reads the skeleton and is lost in the nouns — which is exactly the Level-3 target.

## 2 · The reroot bites — name the reality, do not calque

The **compound ban** (`saharannaise.md` §1) is the sharpest rule of composition: **never build a
Baltic calque for a Saharan reality.** A sandstorm is **ṭāyf**, not a "dust-wind" compound; a
she-camel is **nāg**, not a "milk-cow" of the desert. The reality is *named*, not translated. If you
catch yourself compounding two mainland roots to describe something the desert already has a word for,
stop and use the Saharan root.

- ✅ *ṭāyf tulī* "a sandstorm came" · *nāg donā ləbn* "the she-camel gives soured milk"
- ❌ *tolmô-tūļ* "dust-wind" · *žəml-lehmä* "camel-cow" — mainland bias; corrected to *ṭāyf*, *nāg*.

## 3 · Hybrid morphology — the seam you can use freely

Saharan root + mainland suffix is fully productive (`saharannaise.md` §4). Build agents, places, and
derived verbs on Saharan roots with the ordinary coursebook machinery:

- agent **-ôr**: *žəml* → **žəmlôr** "cameleer" · *māz* → **māzôr** "goatherd" · *xūt* → **xūtôr** "fisher"
- place **-ā**: *bīr* → **bīrā** "a well-site, a waystation with water"
- The join is live and unremarkable — that *is* the thesis. The grammar does not care which sea a root
  came from.

## 4 · Know your tier — write to the intelligibility you intend

Every sentence sits at a transparency tier (`saharannaise.md` §2); good writing is *deliberate* about
which:

- **Level 1 (both read it)** — formal, archival, the learned crust. Law, the manifest, the decree.
  Keep the Romance layer (*reĝistrô, dekretô, provinsô*) and shared borrowings (*girba, xəyma*). A
  mainland clerk reads it.
- **Level 2 (a mainlander manages)** — one content root swapped: *Sūg um sūr* "the market is big"
  (*markôt → sūg*). The frame carries it.
- **Level 3 (a mainlander is lost)** — the domestic core fully rerooted: *Līl um barād* "the night is
  cold." This is home speech, the deep interior. Write here when the scene is domestic and Saharan.

Doublets let you pick your tier word by word: the clerk writes **kanā**, the worker says **sāgī**;
both are canon in their seat (tag **(Metr.)** ↔ **(Sah.)**).

## 5 · A worked paragraph — the caravan reaches the well

Every content root below is archived Saharannaise (lexicons B, C); every frame-word is coursebook.

1. *Gāfila lǟdi erg-läbi, ṣbāx-lt məġrəb-lô.* — "The caravan went through the sand-sea, from morning
   to evening." *(erg, gāfila Saharan; ṣbāx/məġrəb the Saharan day; case-frame -lt … -lô coursebook.)*
2. *Ḍū ūli fort, ja aman ūli petīt.* — "The harsh light was strong, and water was scarce." *(ḍū, aman
   Saharan; um/ūli, fort, petīt coursebook.)*
3. *Məġrəb-al, gāfila kohatai bīrôn.* — "At evening, the caravan reached a well." *(bīr Saharan
   declined with coursebook object **-n**; kohatā "meet" is shared core.)*
4. *Rāžəl kuinai atāyn, ja ahl menjai tamarôn ja ġrumôn.* — "The man made tea, and the household ate
   dates and bread." *(rāžəl, atāy, ahl, tamar, ġrum Saharan; the verbs and object-case coursebook.)*

**Run together:**

> *Gāfila lǟdi erg-läbi, ṣbāx-lt məġrəb-lô. Ḍū ūli fort, ja aman ūli petīt. Məġrəb-al, gāfila kohatai
> bīrôn. Rāžəl kuinai atāyn, ja ahl menjai tamarôn ja ġrumôn.*

The skeleton never drifts — SVO, the copula, the cases, the past **-i** — and the Saharan roots
decline through it like any noun. That is a paragraph of Saharannaise: mainland grammar, Saharan world.

## 6 · When a word is missing — flag it for the Dīwān

If you need a domestic/ecological word Saharannaise does not yet have, **do not** calque it from
Baltic roots and **do not** borrow a Baltic word. Flag it as a gap for the **Kēļs Dīwān** — the Saharan
root is coined by the Register, from Hassaniya or Zenaga, exactly as the coursebook coins Metropolitan
words. (Shared *grammar* words — a new tense, a new case — are never a Saharannaise matter; those are
joint, ruled by the Kolēgi with the Dīwān.) The gap-list of a corpus text is its most valuable output,
here as on the mainland.
