# Saharannaise Reader — *Arriving in Daxla* (a visitor's day, in dialogue)

*Realistic modern dialogue in Saharannaise — the Atlantic (Saharan-coast) counterpart of the
Congolaise `../congolaise/dialogue-arrival.md`, and the Atlanta leg of the coast-wide
`../reader/phrasebook.md`. A visitor's first hours in **Daxla**: the taxi in, a hotel desk, a café.
The modern infrastructure words are the **shared crust** (transparent coast-wide — *taksô, otelô,
pāsportô, wifī, kambôr*); the human exchange is **Saharannaise** (courtesy from lexicon D, food from
lexicon C); the greeting gates each one. Every frame-word is coursebook canon; every content root is
archived Saharannaise.*

---

## Scene 1 · The taxi (terminal → hotel)

> **Visitor.** Salām!
> *peace!* — "Hello!"
>
> **Driver.** Salām. Lābās? Kus-lô?
> *peace. well? where-to?* — "Hello. All well? Where to?"
>
> **Visitor.** Lābās. Otelô Lāyūn-lô. Mis prēu?
> *fine. hotel Lāyūn-to. what price?* — "Fine, thanks. To the Hotel Lāyūn. How much?"
>
> **Driver.** Düna.
> *twelve.* — "Twelve." *(dozenal)*
>
> **Visitor.** Nē — sūr mult. Petīt, afak.
> *no — big much. small, please.* — "No — too much. A bit less, please."
>
> **Driver.** *(laughs)* Yaḷḷa, tulē.
> *come on, come.* — "Alright, get in."
>
> **Visitor.** Barka. Otelô kaug?
> *thanks. hotel far?* — "Thanks. Is the hotel far?"
>
> **Driver.** Nē, prop. Düna minutô, inxāllah.
> *no, near. twelve minutes, God-willing.* — "No, close. Twelve minutes, God willing." *(minutô, crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Sīn, afak. Püsām.
> *here, please. stop.* — "Here, please. Stop."

## Scene 2 · The hotel desk

> **Clerk.** Salām! Marxaba.
> *peace! welcome.* — "Hello, welcome."
>
> **Visitor.** Salām. Mä sōv kambôr — tri pǟvô.
> *peace. I want room — three days.* — "Hello. I'd like a room, three nights."
>
> **Clerk.** Ahlan. Pāsportô, afak.
> *welcome. passport, please.* — "Welcome. Your passport, please." *(pāsportô, crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Sīn. Wifī um?
> *here. wi-fi is?* — "Here. Is there wi-fi?"
>
> **Clerk.** Jā, wifī bxēr. Sīn um vôtī.
> *yes, wi-fi well. here is key.* — "Yes, good wi-fi. Here's your key." *(wifī, vôtī crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Barka. Aman um jôvā?
> *thanks. water is good?* — "Thanks. Is the tap water safe?"
>
> **Clerk.** Nē — sōv aman-butelô. Farmasī um prop, sūg-al.
> *no — want water-bottle. pharmacy is near, market-at.* — "No — get bottled water. There's a
> pharmacy by the market." *(butelô, farmasī crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Barka, ṣaxb.
> *thanks, friend.* — "Thanks a lot, friend."

## Scene 3 · The café

> **Waiter.** Salām! Mis sä sōv?
> *peace! what you want?* — "Hello. What can I get you?"
>
> **Visitor.** Salām. Atāy, ja... mis um jôvā?
> *peace. tea, and… what is good?* — "Hello. A tea, and — what's good?"
>
> **Waiter.** Xūt ja ġrum, ja tamar. Um jôvā sūr.
> *fish and bread, and dates. is good big.* — "Fish and bread, and dates. Very good."
>
> **Visitor.** Jā, afak.
> *yes, please.* — "Yes, please."
>
> *— later —*
>
> **Visitor.** Um jôvā sūr. Barka. Prēu, afak?
> *is good big. thanks. price, please?* — "Excellent. Thank you. The bill, please?"
>
> **Waiter.** Sīn. Rā um kartô jôvā.
> *here. money — card is good.* — "Here. Card is fine." *(kartô, crust)*
>
> **Visitor.** Xukran. Lǟdô b-slāma.
> *thank you. go with-peace.* — "Thank you. Take care."
>
> **Waiter.** Ahl-lô, slām.
> *household-to, peace.* — "Regards to your family."

## What it demonstrates (the coast, mirrored)

- **The same two layers as Ndar.** Infrastructure is the shared crust (*taksô, otelô, pāsportô,
  wifī, kambôr, vôtī, farmasī, kartô, minutô, butelô*) — **identical** to the Congolaise dialogue,
  because it is one coast and one language; the human layer is Saharannaise (*salām, lābās, barka,
  afak, marxaba, xukran, b-slāma*, and the food *atāy, xūt, ġrum, tamar*).
- **The greeting still gates everything**, and it is "peace" here too — *Salām* answers *Jam* across
  the water, the same instinct in a different tongue.
- **The spine carries it.** *Kus-lô?*, *Mis prēu?*, *Wifī um?*, negation *nē/äb*, the cases
  (*Lāyūn-lô*, *sūg-al*, *ahl-lô*), the dozenal *düna* — all coursebook, the same as on the mainland
  and the river.
- **Set the two dialogues side by side** and the thesis is plain: swap *salām↔jam*, *barka↔jārāma*,
  *atāy↔kafē*, *xūt↔jën*, *sūg↔lumo*, *b-slāma↔jam-k* — and the taxi, the desk, the café, the grammar,
  and the whole modern crust stay exactly where they are. One language, two coasts.
