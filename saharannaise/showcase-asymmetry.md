# Showcase — one scene, two standards

The asymmetric-intelligibility standard (`saharannaise.md` §2), *shown*. Each line is the
same sentence in **Metropolitan Nelôxi** and **Saharannaise**. The structure is identical
in both — same word order, same copula *um* "is", same negation *äb*, same dozenal numbers
— so the skeleton is always transparent. What changes is the content roots, and how many
of them change is exactly what tier the line sits in. That is the whole thesis in a page:
**the grammar never drifts; the lexicon does all the diverging.**

Read the Metropolitan line as the mainland clerk writes it; read the Saharannaise line as
the Daxla dockworker says it.

---

## Level 1 — formal & archival (both sides read it)

A manifest line. The referents are Saharan objects the mainland has **no native word for**
(tent, waterskin, caravan), so both standards carry the same borrowed roots — the loan is
shared, and the line is transparent to everyone.

> **Metr.**  Düna girba, tri xəyma.
> **Sah.**  Düna girba, tri xəyma.
> *twelve waterskin, three tent* — "Twelve waterskins, three tents."

Identical. This is Level 1: shared frame, shared (borrowed) content. The mainland gap is
filled by the Saharan word in **both** registers — the mechanism by which back-flow begins.

## Level 2 — one root swapped (a mainlander manages)

> **Metr.**  Markôt um sūr.
> **Sah.**  Sūg um sūr.
> *market is big* — "The market is big."

The frame *um sūr* is shared and instant; only **markôt → sūg** differs. A mainlander with
a little exposure recovers it. A Saharannaise speaker, schooled in the standard, reads the
Metropolitan line with no trouble at all — the asymmetry runs one way.

## Level 3 — the reroot bites (a mainlander is lost)

> **Metr.**  Nahtô um kylm.
> **Sah.**  Līl um barād.
> *night is cold* — "The night is cold."

Same skeleton, but now **both** content roots have gone Saharan: *nahtô → līl* "night",
*kylm → barād* — and *barād* is not just "cold," it is the specific desert cold of winter
nights (Batch 1). Two swaps and the copula is the only thing a mainlander still recognizes.
This is Level 3: the domestic layer fully rerooted, near-zero intelligibility outward.

## Back-flow — the loan travels home

> **both:**  Gāfila tulē.
> *caravan comes* — "The caravan comes."

"Caravan" is a mainland gap (`gāfila`, Batch 2). As Atlanta's overland trade feeds the
Atlantic ports, the Saharan word rides back into metropolitan speech — first as the only
available term, then as a prestige maritime loan. Flagged for a future mainland batch; it
becomes coursebook canon only by a Rector merge.

---

*All Metropolitan forms here (um, äb, sūr, kaug, kylm, nahtô, markôt, tulē, düna, tri) are
coursebook canon; all Saharannaise content roots are archived in Batches 1–2.*
