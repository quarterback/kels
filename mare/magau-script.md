# The Magau Mixed Script — hanzi roots, Latin bones

*The script standard of **Magau Maré** — the Meridian territory of Magau (`../world/meridian-states.md`)
writing its own dialect in mixed **hanzi + Latin**. A script overlay only: same Nelôxi, same Maré
grammar (`mare.md`), same words as the Latin-written form. Nothing here changes the language;
it changes what the page looks like.*

## 1 · The governing principle

**Mix scripts when the target script cannot carry the grammar.** Hanzi are morphemic — they can
write a *root*, but they cannot spell an agglutinative case ending, the negation *äb*, or a tense
particle. So the two scripts divide labor, exactly as Japanese divides kanji and kana:

| Layer | Script | Why |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Sinitic-sourced content roots** (the Cantonese fill of Magau Maré) | **hanzi** (traditional set, as Magau has always carved them) | each root already *is* a character; the etymology is the spelling |
| **the entire grammatical spine** — case endings, the deck particles *ti / fin / kē*, *äb*, the copula *um/ūli*, pronouns, connectives, derivational suffixes, the dozenal numerals | **Latin** | hanzi cannot represent it |
| **every non-Sinitic root** — the Nelôxi core (*mer, tūļ, xipd*), the crust (*otelô, politsī*), the other territories' loans (*bāi, mālô, guagua, lēve-lēve*) | **Latin** | see §3 |

On the page you *see* the architecture of the dialect: **Sinitic skin, Nelôxi bones.** The model
is Japanese with Latin in the kana role; the cousins are written Cantonese and Korean hanja.

## 2 · Which words take hanzi

A word is written in hanzi **if and only if its root is Sinitic-sourced** — the Cantonese layer of
Magau Maré. The character is part of the word's lexical entry, archived once, invariant thereafter
(no free variation; the Three-Watch archive records the hanzi beside the romanization):

| Hanzi | Latin | Gloss | Standing |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| 早晨 | **jôsan** | good morning (the harbor greeting) | terr. |
| 好 | **hou** | good, fine, OK | terr. |
| 唔該 | **mgōi** | thanks / excuse me | terr. |
| 舢舨 | **sampan** | harbor skiff | terr. |
| 颱風 | **taifūn** | the great named storm | **realm canon** (Magau the owning seat) |
| 碼頭 | **mātau** | the city waterfront, the quay-front | terr. |
| 茶 | **çā** | tea, the local leaf (beside the crust's *tē*) | terr. |
| 買 | **māi** | to buy | terr. |
| 馬交 | **Magau** | the territory itself (the historical characters) | proper noun |

*Realm-canon words of Magau origin (taifūn) keep their hanzi **in Magau writing only**; the rest
of the realm writes them in Latin like everything else. The script overlay travels no further than
the territory, exactly as Uusatôm's Cyrillic stays on the Black Sea.*

## 3 · Non-Sinitic roots stay Latin — confirmed, and why

The tempting shortcut is the Japanese *kun*-reading: write 海 and pronounce it *mer*. **Forbidden.**
In this standard a character spells a *Sinitic etymology*, never a *meaning* — the moment 海 can be
read *mer*, the script stops showing the dialect's architecture and starts hiding it, and every
reader needs a reading-dictionary. One rule keeps the whole system learnable: **see hanzi, hear
Cantonese-sourced Maré; see Latin, hear everything else.** No exceptions, including proper nouns
(non-Sinitic names stay Latin; 馬交 is legal because the name is Sinitic to begin with).

## 4 · The seam — how the scripts meet on the line

- **Words are spaced normally.** Nelôxi is a space-separated language and stays one in mixed
  script (unlike Japanese, which has kana to mark the joints).
- **A bound suffix attaches at the hanzi with a hyphen:** 碼頭-lô "to the quay-front," 舢舨-ôr
  "skiffman" (agent *-ôr*), 茶-t "some tea" (partitive). The hyphen is the deck register's
  sanded-case habit (*satām-lô*) doing double duty as the script seam — you always know where the
  root ends and the spine begins.
- **Free grammatical words stand alone in Latin** exactly as usual: *äb* 買 "doesn't buy," *ti* 買
  "bought," 舢舨 *kē lǟdô* "the skiff will go."
- **Direction:** horizontal, left-to-right only — the line must interleave with Latin.
- **Register:** the mixed script is Magau's local written form for deck and territorial writing.
  **Paper and Federal Maré on Magau stay Latin** — manifests, courts, and the CMT record are
  realm-wide documents, and the realm reads Latin.

## 5 · Worked passage — the quay at Magau

Four sentences of Magau Maré: mixed script, then the Latin-only line (identical language), then
English.

> **早晨, bāi! 舢舨 kē lǟdô 碼頭-lô?**
> *Jôsan, bāi! Sampan kē lǟdô mātau-lô?*
> "Morning, mate! Is the skiff heading over to the quay-front?"
>
> **好. Mä ti 買 茶 markôt-al — sä sōv 茶-t?**
> *Hou. Mä ti māi çā markôt-al — sä sōv çā-t?*
> "Fine. I bought tea at the market — want some tea?"
>
> **颱風 kē tulē demā; mer äb selgô.**
> *Taifūn kē tulē demā; mer äb selgô.*
> "A typhoon's coming tomorrow; the sea is not clear."
>
> **Tôl 舢舨-ôr saistā satām-al. 唔該, bāi — tūļ jôvā!**
> *Tôl sampanôr saistā satām-al. Mgōi, bāi — tūļ jôvā!*
> "Then the skiffman stays in harbor. Thanks, mate — fair wind!"

Read the mixed lines with §1's rule and the dialect's whole anatomy is visible at a glance: every
character is the Cantonese fill (greeting, tea, skiff, storm, buying); every Latin letter is either
the Nelôxi spine (*kē, ti, äb, -lô, -al, -ôr, -t, mä, sä, tôl*) or a non-Sinitic root (*bāi* from
Bermuda, *mer/tūļ/satām* from the Baltic core, *markôt* from the crust, *selgô* from the deck
register). The Latin-only line underneath is not a translation — it is **the same sentence**, which
is the entire point.

## 6 · Guardrails

- **No grammar changes, no vocabulary changes.** Spoken Magau Maré is identical however written.
- **One spelling per word:** the hanzi of each Sinitic root is fixed in its lexical entry; a word
  not in the archive is written in Latin until the entry rules its character.
- The sound inventory is the coursebook's plus the Maré conventions (`mare.md` §4); this overlay
  maps none of it — hanzi spell roots whole, and everything phonological stays Latin.

The dialect under this script is sketched in `dialects-phrasebook.md` (Magau rows); the standard it
