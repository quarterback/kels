# The Six Standards — Comparison Chart

*Metropolitan Nelôxi · Saharannaise · Congolaise · Verdenese · the Sarmatian standard — with
**Meralian**, the Meridian daughter language, beside them — laid side by side.* The chart
lineage: the three-standard chart (`../congolaise/comparison-chart.md`) showed identical grammar
under three divergent lexicons; the ocean chart (`../mare/comparison-chart.md`) now shows the
daughter language that outgrew the deck register; the spine showcase
(`../verdenese/showcase-pentalect.md`) added the settler saturation. This chart adds the Sarmatian
fork and completes the grid — because the sixth standard diverges in the one dimension none of
the first five touched: **all of the words kept, all of the grammatical lead handed over.**

---

## 0 · The grid — who keeps what

| Standard | The words | The grammar | The move | A mainlander is lost in… |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Metropolitan** | the trunk (five quarries over a Livonian core) | the trunk | — | — |
| **Saharannaise** | domestic core rerooted (Hassaniya/Zenaga) | invariant | reroot one substrate | the words (Level 3 opaque) |
| **Congolaise** | domestic core fused (Pulaar/Soninke/Wolof) | invariant | fuse three tributaries | the words |
| **Meralian** | Neloxian roots, reshaped to open syllables (*satām→satama*) | replaced — Carolinian-shaped daughter grammar | leave home | both — a daughter language, not a co-standard |
| **Verdenese** | Romance-saturated + Guaraní land-layer | invariant | saturate a settler crucible | the words (half-guessable) |
| **Sarmatian standard** | **Metropolitan word-stock, whole** | **Circassian-led, wholly different** | **invert the layering** | **the structure** |

Read it as proof in three directions: the spine-standards vary the *words* column and hold the
grammar; Meralian varied both and became a daughter language; the Sarmatian standard holds the
words and varies the *grammar* column alone. One lexicon shown surviving any structure; one
structure shown surviving any lexicon; one child shown outliving both.

## 1 · The structural contrast — feature by feature

The heart of the chart. Because Saharannaise, Congolaise, and Verdenese run the Metropolitan
frame exactly, they share its column; the Sarmatian standard gets its own — and Meralian's
daughter profile is set beside them for contrast (attested cells from `../mare/mare.md`; "—"
where the daughter's profile does not specify).

| Feature | Metropolitan (= Sah. = Cong. = Verd.) | Meralian (the ocean daughter) | Sarmatian standard |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Word order | SVO, fixed | **predicate-first**, focus fronting | **SOV — verb-final** |
| Alignment | nominative–accusative | none — no case; role by order & particles | **ergative — agent takes -r** |
| Object marking | object **-n**, partitive **-t** | none — cases gone | **unmarked (absolutive); -n retired** |
| Person on the verb | none — verbs isolating | none; free pronouns (incl./excl., dual, paucal) | **obligatory agreement prefixes (ABS + ERG), role read from slot order** |
| Direction | adverbs and cases | **grammatical directionals: mai/atu, loto/tai…** | **preverbs packed into the verb: sin-, tôl-, maha-** |
| Negation | pre-verbal particle **äb** | its own particles (not *äb*) | **verb-internal suffix -p; äb does not exist** |
| Past tense | suffix **-i**, two classes | **particle ti** (from the deck register) | **-i kept**, riding inside the verb complex |
| Completion / prospect | object's case / time-words | **fin / kē** particles | time-words (partitive aspect retired) |
| Possession | **Mäl um rā** (locative + copula) | **possessive classifiers: ka/nu/to/va/ra/na** | **Rā m-um** — packed into an agreeing copula |
| Goal case | **-lô/-lä**, harmonized | none — directionals serve | **-le** invariant (the Estonian allative) |
| Postpositions | ground in linking **-s** (*kōtôs al*) | relational particles | ground in oblique **-m** (*kōtôm al*) |
| Counted nouns | partitive (*tri kalāt*) | **numeral classifiers** (*rua va xipa*) | **bare** (*tri kalā*) |
| Word shapes | closed syllables, clusters, macrons | **open syllables — roots reshaped (xipd→xipa)** | **unchanged — letter for letter** |
| Complement clause | *et* + clause, after the verb | — | **clause + et, before the verb** |
| Relative clause | participle or *ken/mis* clause | — | **prenominal participle primary** |
| Adj–N · Num–N · Gen–N · postpositional NP | head-final NP | — | **kept — identical** |
| Derivation, dozenal numbers, harmony, gradation | the shared machinery | reduplication productive; the rest its own | **kept — identical** |

Two things to notice. Meralian's column is another language's profile — the daughter kept the
roots and replaced the machine *and* the shapes. The Sarmatian column differs in **most of the
clause rows and none of the phrase rows** — the noun phrase, the word-building machinery, and
the word-shapes themselves are untouched, which is why every Sarmatian sentence is built of
parts a mainlander already owns.

## 2 · One scene, six ways — the lines that show it

From the six-way showcase (`showcase-hexalect.md`), the two most diagnostic rows:

**"The night is cold"** — the domestic layer, where lexical divergence is deepest:

| | |
|:---|:---|
| **Metr.** | Nahtô um kylm. |
| **Sah.** | Līl um barād. |
| **Cong.** | Jemma um ɓūɓ. |
| **Verd.** | Notē um frīu. |
| **Sarm.** | **Nahtô kylm um.** |

The sisters go dark word by word; the Sarmatian line keeps Metropolitan's letters and moves one.

**"Yesterday I went to the market"** — the grammar row, where structural divergence is deepest:

| | |
|:---|:---|
| **Metr.** | Ajēr mä lǟdi markôtôlô. |
| **Sah.** | Ajēr mä lǟdi sūg-lô. |
| **Cong.** | Ajēr mä lǟdi lumo-lô. |
| **Verd.** | Ajēr mä lǟdi ferjā-lô. |
| **Mrl.** | *Ti lēda au.* — the daughter's own clause |
| **Sarm.** | Ajēr mä markôtôle **me-lǟdi**. |

Four standards swap the noun and hold the frame; Meralian rebuilt clause, shapes, and all; the
Sarmatian standard holds the noun untouched and rebuilds only the clause — goal leveled to *-le*,
verb to the end, the subject echoed into it as a prefix.

## 3 · The lexical layering — same architecture, sixth loadout

Every standard is the same four-layer building with different tenants:

| Standard | Domestic core | Casual loan layer | Land / realia layer | Learned crust |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Metropolitan** | Baltic-native | **Low German** (*melk, moin, dankô*) | — | Romance-Latin |
| **Saharannaise** | Hassaniya/Zenaga (rerooted) | *(inside the reroot)* | desert realia (*ṭāyf, gāfila*) | Romance kept |
| **Congolaise** | three-tributary fusion | *(inside the fusion)* | river realia (*wālo, jeli, kora*) | Romance kept |
| **Meralian** | Neloxian roots, reshaped (*satama, xipa, livu*) | the territorial layers (Canarian, Forro, Cantonese…) | ocean realia (*taifūn, motū*) | inherited crust, reshaped |
| **Verdenese** | Romance-saturated | *(the saturation is the casual layer)* | Guaraní land-layer (*jaguaretē, kaʼaguy*) | Romance kept |
| **Sarmatian standard** | **Metropolitan stock, unchanged** | **Turkic** (*para, bazar, txai, dost, haidē*) | eastern realia (*txorbā, txarxô, mangāl*) | Romance kept |

The Sarmatian standard's row is the tell: its casual layer sits in **exactly the Low German slot** —
*para* beside *rā* as *melk* beside *lēt* — which is why its doublets feel native to the
system even though its clauses do not.

## 4 · Signature forms — each seat's unmistakable line

The first five standards are known by their *words*; the Sarmatian standard is known by its *shapes*:

| Only in… | Signature | Gloss |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Saharannaise** | **žəml · gāfila · ṭāyf** | camel · caravan · sandstorm |
| **Congolaise** | **jeli · terānga · kora** | griot · the guest-honor ethic · the harp-lute |
| **Meralian** | **ti / fin / kē · ka/nu/to/va/ra/na · kita/kami** | the particle chain · the possessive classifiers · we-inclusive / we-exclusive |
| **Verdenese** | **jaguaretē · matē · saudādē** | the jaguar · the gourd · the ache |
| **Sarmatian standard** | **Kalāji-r kalā vendā.** | the ergative *-r* — coursebook words, nobody else's clause |
| **Sarmatian standard** | **Me-nǟgô-p.** | "I don't see it" — subject, object, and negation in one word |
| **Sarmatian standard** | **Rā m-um.** | "I have money" — possession packed into two syllables of agreeing copula |

Back-flow rides the shared convention: **txai (Sarm.→Metr.)** and **haidē (Sarm.→Metr.)** are the
live candidates, drifting up through Uusatôm — but note what *cannot* back-flow: the grammar.
*-r*, *-p*, and the packed verb are namespaced to `sarmatian/` by design; a doublet can climb the
trunk, a clause structure cannot.

## 5 · How to read the six-way divergence

- **Lexical rows:** Metropolitan and the **Sarmatian standard** pair up — two seats, one
  word-stock, letter for letter; Meralian's stock is Neloxian by descent but reshaped past
  string-identity; Saharannaise, Congolaise, and Verdenese each stand alone.
- **Grammar rows:** Metropolitan, Saharannaise, Congolaise, and Verdenese pair up; Meralian
  stands in its own language; the **Sarmatian standard stands entirely alone inside Nelôxi**.
- **The asymmetry always runs the same way** — the periphery reads the metropole better than
  the reverse — but for two different reasons: everywhere else the mainlander drowns in
  unknown words inside a known machine; in the east they float on known words inside an
  unknown one.
- **The gaps still carry the history.** The desert has no sea, the river no atoll, the Baltic
  no typhoon, the pampa no caravan — and only the east needed its grammar to shake hands with
  the Caucasus.
