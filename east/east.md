# The East fork — the Idā standard of Nelôxi (provisional)

*Idā kēļ, the East speech.* The Nelôxi of **East Neloxia** — the eastern continental span of
the old state, from the far Black Sea shore through the Caucasus gates toward the Caspian and
the Herat road: a **separate sovereign country**, Neloxia-aligned, separated from Kunislinnô
**amicably** (too much land to administer from the metropole, rich enough to stand alone),
with **Nelôxi as its business and commercial language** — not English, not Russian. Its
trade corridor runs Black Sea (Crimea) → Caspian → Persian Gulf, and at the far end the two
states jointly hold the **Kuwait condominium**, the Hormuz berth where the split-off halves
of the Nelôxi-speaking world touch hands (`../world/kuwait-condominium.md`). This is the
**sixth fork** of the pluricentric language, and it is unlike the other five in exactly
one inverted way, set out below.

> **Status of the name.** The country is provisionally **East Neloxia**, its independence-era
> name. It may keep that name or take an amalgamated one — candidates under consideration:
> **Colchia / Kôlxônô, Sarmatia, Alania, Šaraya, Kervania**. Until the founder rules, this
> section calls the standard **the East fork** (Nelôxi tag: **(Id.)**, from *idā* "east" —
> provisional like everything else named here). Nothing in this section pre-empts the ruling;
> the linguistics stand whatever the flag ends up saying.

## Why it exists — the inversion thesis

Every other standard runs the same experiment in the same direction: **the Livonian-Karelian
grammar is the invariant spine, and the region supplies vocabulary.** Saharannaise reroots the
domestic lexicon into Hassaniya and Zenaga; Congolaise fuses three Niger-Congo tributaries;
Verdenese saturates in Romance and borrows the land from Guaraní; even Maré, which bends
grammar, bends it in one bounded, jointly-ratified place and keeps the Metropolitan
word-stock. In all five, a Metropolitan speaker **knows the grammar and is lost in the words**.

The East fork is the only fork where the Finnic element is **not the grammatical lead**. Here
the **regional language leads the grammar** — Circassian (Adyghe), the indigenous language of
the Caucasus gates — and the Finnic partner is demoted to second chair. The result is the same
Nelôxi lexicon, restructured into something that patterns like a Caucasian language: verb-final,
ergative, the verb a heavy word that packs subject, object, direction, and negation into
itself. A Metropolitan speaker **knows every word and is lost in the structure** — the exact
inverse of the other five forks.

This is the sixth proof of the sturdiness thesis, and the one the first five could not give.
Saharannaise proved the skeleton can carry any lexicon; the East fork proves the lexicon can
ride any skeleton — that the family can be a **partner in another language's structure**, not
only the skeleton of its own. A cipher survives neither test. Nelôxi survives both.

## The one rule — read this first

**The core vocabulary is Nelôxi.** The regional languages contribute **grammar and a loan
layer, not the core words.** A speaker must see the familiar Nelôxi words — *mer, turg, lǟdô,
nǟgô, rā, amīk, mä, tä* — and recognize them. No Circassian or other Caucasian root may
substitute for a core lexeme; the core lexicon is the same Nelôxi as every other standard.
This is the rule that was nearly broken in early drafting, and the East fork's review process
enforces it the way the Dīwān enforces the compound ban: a submission that swaps a Caucasian
content-root into a slot canon already fills is **rejected — regional bias**, the mirror image
of Saharannaise's *rejected — mainland bias*.

## The layering (precise)

1. **Vocabulary = Nelôxi.** The core lexicon, unchanged — the words the language already has.
   Where the East fork needs a word canon lacks, it coins by the shared derivational machinery
   or borrows from its own loan quarry (layer 4), never from the grammar-lead language.
2. **Grammar lead = Circassian (Adyghe).** Sentence structure follows Circassian patterns:
   SOV, ergative alignment, the polysynthetic verb, verb-internal negation. The full spec is
   `grammar-circassian.md`.
3. **Secondary Finnic partner = Estonian.** Chosen as the most evolved, most completely
   codified Finnic option. Its role is the **noun morphology** — the case endings — and the
   thread back to the Neloxian family: felt more than seen. It must **not** become the master
   grammar; the fence that keeps the fork from drifting into Estonian is drawn in
   `noun-cases.md` §5.
4. **Loan quarry = Turkic (Turkish-type),** in the exact structural role Low German plays in
   Metropolitan: the heaviest source of everyday, casual borrowed vocabulary, sitting as
   doublets beside the native core words (*para* beside *rā*, *bazar* beside *turg*). Turkic
   leads the **loan layer, not the grammar** — the pan-Turkic bloc next door already owns the
   Turkic identity, and leading with Turkic grammar would blur the two states; Turkic as
   loan-quarry reflects the ambient regional trade language realistically. Lighter sources
   where they fit: **Persian/Khorasani** at the eastern (Herat) end, **Genoese-Italian** trade
   residue at the Crimean end. The quarry is `loans-turkic.md`.

## The intelligibility profile — the asymmetry, inverted

The other forks' tiers run on vocabulary: the deeper the register, the darker the words. The
East fork's tiers run on **structure**, and so the divergence is visible at *every* level —
even the Level-1 archival line, identical across the other five standards, comes back
rearranged from the East (see `showcase-hexalect.md`).

- **Reading, mainland → East:** a Metropolitan reader recognizes every content word on the
  page and can usually reconstruct the proposition — the words are a complete gloss of
  themselves. What defeats the reader is live parsing: who did what to whom (the ergative),
  and what the verb's prefixes are carrying.
- **Reading, East → mainland:** effortless. An East-fork speaker schooled in the business
  register reads the Metropolitan page the way a Meridian reads it — the words are the same
  and the Metropolitan structure is the simpler machine. The asymmetry runs the same
  direction as everywhere else in the language; only its mechanism is new.
- **Speech:** a mainlander hears East fork as *Nelôxi words in the wrong order, fused into
  long verbs* — followable in fragments, lost in clauses. String-identical sentences do occur
  (subject + intransitive verb: *Kalāji tulē* "the fisher is coming" is both standards at
  once), and East-fork speakers use them deliberately as courtesy register with mainland
  guests.

## Governance

- Seat: proposed as the **Kēļs Xasā** (the Assembly of the Language — Adyghe *хасэ* "council,
  assembly," the institution-noun of the grammar-lead language, taken through the shared §47
  friction rule *kh → x* exactly as *dīwān* and *penc* were taken from theirs), headed by
  **the Tamadā** (the Elder of the Table — the pan-Caucasian presiding elder, a word the
  whole region shares). *Proposal only; the founder may rename, as with the Kēļs Penc and
  Kēļs Vakt.*
- The Kēļs Xasā governs the East-fork lexicon (the Turkic loan layer and any East coinage)
  and the fork's grammar spec. The **grammar divergence is the fork's own, whole and
  namespaced**: unlike Maré's Maritime Amendment it is not a bounded bend inside the shared
  spine but a separate structural standard, legal only under `east/`. No other seat may
  adopt any of it; the East fork may not export any of it; the shared spine of the other
  five standards (`../saharannaise/COMMON-REGISTER.md`) is untouched.
- Region tags extend the Common Register convention: **(Id.)** East-fork-only; **(Id.→Metr.)**
  a back-flow candidate drifting up the trade routes — the live candidates are *txai* and
  *haidē*, both entering through **Uusatôm**, the Black Sea contact port between the two
  standards. Back-flow becomes Metropolitan canon only by Rector merge, never automatically.

## Accounting

East-fork material is counted **on its own terms**, in its own namespace, like every peer
standard: nothing here enters `data/*`, the charter §7 list, or the coursebook. The lint gate
(`tools/regen_data.py`) reads only Metropolitan canon; the East fork adds **no new graphemes**
to any inventory (see `script.md`), and its own two drift rules — no Caucasian roots in core
slots, no Estonian citation-shapes in coinage — are enforced by Xasā review, not by the gate.

## Deviations from the build brief, recorded

Per the coordination guardrail (the Uusatôm precedent: canon wins over a brief's
illustrations, and the deviation is written down):

1. **Canon word-forms.** The brief's illustrative lexemes are corrected to canon: *tōrgü* →
   **turg** "market(place)"; *nǟg/nǟn* → **nǟgô** "to see"; *lǟn* (as "go") → **lǟdô** — in
   canon *lǟn* is "west"; *sõbrô* → **amīk** "friend" (*sõbrô* is not canon and carries the
   retired stem-*õ*). *mer, rā, mä, tä* stand as given.
2. **Agreement affixes.** The brief sketched Circassian-shaped affixes (1sg *sᵉ-*, object
   *nᵉ-*). Systematized instead from the **Nelôxi pronouns** (*mä, sä, tä, mēg, tēg, ne* →
   *me-, se-, te-, mēg-, tēg-, ne-*): affix material is vocabulary-like, and the one rule
   says the material is Nelôxi — Circassian supplies the *slots and their order*, not the
   phonological stuff. (The brief's *sᵉ-* "I" would also read as Nelôxi second person.) The
   brief's verb-internal negation **-p** is kept exactly — it is the Adyghe negative *-ep*
   pattern, a structural borrowing, and structure is what Circassian is here to give.
3. **The -r/-m contrast.** Ergative **-r** follows the brief's worked examples; **-m** is
   assigned the postpositional-oblique role (`noun-cases.md`). Real Adyghe distributes the
   pair the other way around; the brief's assignment is kept and the difference recorded.
