# Dialogue — At the Bazar (*Bazarôl*)

*The East fork's everyday register in living use: a customer at a tea-and-bread stall in the
covered market. Casual speech, so the Turkic doublets are out (`loans-turkic.md`) and the
verbs are packed (`grammar-circassian.md` §4). Every core root is coursebook canon; a
Metropolitan reader should test the fork's thesis here — gloss every word, then feel the
structure push back. The sibling texts are `../saharannaise/dialogue-arrival.md` and
`../congolaise/dialogue-arrival.md`.*

---

**A** (customer) · **B** (stall-keeper, a *dükānôr* in the *txarxô*)

> **A:** Moin, dost! Txai kalēnt um-ka?
> *hello friend | tea hot be-Q*
> "Morning, friend! Is the tea hot?"

> **B:** Kalēnt um, kalēnt. Sin-tulē, sin-tulē!
> *hot be, hot | hither-come, hither-come*
> "It's hot, it's hot. Come here, come on over!"

> **A:** Dva txai, bidô. Ekmek s-um-ka?
> *two tea, please | bread 2sg-have-Q*
> "Two teas, please. Do you have bread?"

> **B:** Ekmek m-um-p, agā nān m-um — ja txorbā txok jôvā um.
> *bread 1sg-have-NEG, but nan 1sg-have — and soup very good be*
> "No bread, but I've got nan — and the soup is very good."

> **A:** Haidē — nān ja txorbā. Kui mult um?
> *come-on — nan and soup | how much be*
> "Go on then — nan and soup. How much is it?"

> **B:** Tri para, dost.
> *three para, friend*
> "Three para, friend."

> **A:** Tri?! Dva para — yavax-yavax, dost!
> *three?! two para — easy-easy, friend*
> "Three?! Two para — easy now, friend!"

> **B:** Yavax, yavax… jôvā. Dva para — kôsmet um!
> *easy, easy… good | two para — fate be*
> "Alright, alright… fine. Two para — that's kismet!"

> **A:** Mô-komprā. Se para — votā!
> *1sg.ERG-buy | this money — take*
> "I'll take it. Here's the money — go on!"

> **B:** Dankô, dost! Ja haidē, sōvā-p — txorbā jubā kalēnt um!
> *thanks friend | and come-on, wish-NEG — soup already hot be*
> "Thanks, friend! And come on, don't stand there wanting — the soup's already hot!"

## What the dialogue shows

- **Every verb last, every question on the verb.** *Txai kalēnt um-ka?* — the polar *ka*
  encliticized to the clause-final copula (§2 of the grammar spec); Metropolitan would open
  with it (*Ka tē um kalēnt?*).
- **Possession packed** (§7): *s-um-ka?* "have you?", *m-um-p* "I don't have", *m-um* "I
  have" — the whole Metropolitan *Mäl um X* frame folded into three syllables of agreeing
  copula.
- **Negation verb-internal, no *äb* anywhere**: *m-um-p*, *sōvā-p* — including the negative
  imperative (§5).
- **The agreement prefixes carrying the persons**: *mô-komprā* "I('ll) buy" is a complete
  transitive sentence, agent inside the verb, 3sg object ∅ — one word where Metropolitan
  needs three (*mä komprā sen*).
- **Counted nouns bare** (`noun-cases.md` §3): *dva txai, tri para* — no counted partitive.
- **The doublets doing register work**: *txai, ekmek, nān, txorbā, para, dost, haidē,
  yavax, kôsmet* are the bazar's casual layer; the same stall's ledger would write *tē, pa,
  sup, rā, amīk* — and the greeting *moin*, Low German on the mainland quays, is at home in
  both.
- **String-identical moments**: *Txorbā jubā kalēnt um* is one reordering away from
  Metropolitan, and *Dankô, dost!* needs no translation at all — the courtesy edge of the
  inversion thesis: nothing here is ever lexically foreign to the mainland, only
  structurally so.
