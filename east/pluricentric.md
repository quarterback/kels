# The East Fork and the Six-Center Shape of Nelôxi

Five standards deep, Nelôxi's pluricentric experiment had one axis left untested. Lay the
forks out on a grid — who keeps the words, who keeps the grammar — and the pattern is
lopsided. Metropolitan holds both. Saharannaise, Congolaise, and Verdenese keep the grammar
invariant and change the words: one substrate rerooted, three tributaries fused, a settler
crucible saturated. Maré keeps the words and bends the grammar — but bounded, three bends,
jointly ratified, closed. Nobody had kept **all** the words and handed over the **whole**
grammatical lead. That corner of the grid is the East fork, and it is the corner that
finishes the proof.

The finish matters because of what the first five proofs share. Each of them treats the
Livonian-Karelian skeleton as the invariant — the thing that makes Nelôxi Nelôxi — and
demonstrates its sturdiness by loading foreign freight onto it. The demonstration is real,
but it leaves a suspicion standing: perhaps the skeleton is the *only* real thing here, and
the lexicon just paint. The East fork answers by running the experiment upside down. Take
the paint and repaint someone else's house: the same Nelôxi lexicon — *mer, turg, rā, kalāji,
donā, um*, every word a mainlander already owns — restructured by **Circassian**, the
indigenous grammar of the Caucasus gates. Verb to the end of the clause. The agent marked
ergative. The verb swollen into the heavy word, packing subject, object, direction, and
negation into itself, *äb* gone entirely. A Metropolitan speaker reads an East-fork page the
way the Daxla dockworker's mainland cousin hears the market: every word familiar, the whole
thing foreign. **The other five forks lose the mainlander in the words; the sixth loses them
in the structure.** If the lexicon can survive that — stay recognizably itself while a
Caucasian machine runs it — then the words were never paint either. The family can be a
partner in another language's structure, not only the skeleton of its own.

The internal politics of the fork are a study in restraint, three restraints deep. The
grammar lead went to Circassian, but the affix *material* stayed Nelôxi — the person prefixes
are the fork's own pronouns ground down, the directionals its own adverbs — so that even
inside the heavy verb a mainlander is looking at Nelôxi stuff in Caucasian slots. The Finnic
element was demoted but not dismissed: **Estonian**, the family's most evolved codification,
keeps the second chair and supplies the noun cases — leveled, single-shaped, the goal on
*-le* — the thread back to the family, felt more than seen, and fenced hard so it never
climbs back to the master's chair (the drift-into-Estonian failure mode is the one the fork's
review watches closest). And **Turkic**, the obvious regional heavyweight, was deliberately
seated at the loan-quarry table instead of the grammar table — the pan-Turkic bloc next door
already owns the Turkic identity, and a state whose business language borrowed Turkic
structure would blur into its neighbor; a state that haggles in *para* and *bazar* and *txai*
while writing *rā* and *turg* and *tē* in the ledger merely sounds like where it is. Turkic
in the East fork is Low German in Metropolitan: the casual doublet layer, exactly that deep,
no deeper — with Persian coloring the Herat end and two Genoese trade-words rusting on the
Crimean quays.

What results is the strangest intelligibility profile in the system. The other forks'
asymmetries are tiered by register — the deeper the hearth, the darker the words, and the
Level-1 decree reads identically in five seats. The East fork's divergence is structural, so
it is *everywhere*: even the archival line comes back rearranged, and yet no line ever goes
lexically dark. A mainlander can gloss an entire East-fork paragraph word by word and still
have to work for every proposition; an East-fork clerk reads the mainland page effortlessly,
because the words are the same and the Metropolitan machine is the simpler one. The
asymmetry still runs the direction it runs everywhere — the periphery reads the metropole
better than the reverse — but for the inverse reason, and that inversion is the fork's whole
signature: **same words, different structure**, where every sibling is same structure,
different words.

The country holding the standard is the newest sovereignty in the family's orbit — **East
Neloxia**, provisionally so called: the eastern continental span that separated from
Kunislinnô amicably, too much land to administer from the metropole and rich enough to stand
alone, keeping Nelôxi as its language of business against the pull of every larger neighbor.
(The name itself is still the founder's to rule — Colchia, Sarmatia, Alania, and others stand
on the shortlist — and this section styles it the East fork until then.) Its proposed seat,
the **Kēļs Xasā** under the **Tamadā**, takes its institution-noun from the grammar-lead
language exactly as the Dīwān and the Penc took theirs — the one Circassian word in a fork
that is otherwise, and by its founding rule, Nelôxi to the last root.

Six centers now, and the grid is full: reroot the words (Saharannaise), fuse the words
(Congolaise), saturate the words (Verdenese), sand the grammar's edge (Maré), hold the trunk
(Metropolitan) — and now invert the whole layering (the East fork). One lexicon shown to
survive any structure; one structure shown to survive any lexicon. Whatever else a
pluricentric conlang can be asked to demonstrate, this family has now demonstrated it in
both directions.
