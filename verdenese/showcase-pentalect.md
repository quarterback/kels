# Showcase — one scene, the spine-standards and the daughter

The sturdiness thesis at its fullest stretch: the same lines in **Metropolitan Nelôxi**,
**Saharannaise**, **Congolaise**, and **Verdenese** — the spine-standards — with **Meralian**,
the Meridian daughter language, shown beside them as the relative that left home. Among the
spine-standards the structure is identical — same word order, same copula *um*, same negation
*äb*, same dozenal numbers, same object case — so the skeleton is always transparent, and only
the content roots diverge. Meralian's lines mark the other outcome: the same inherited roots,
carried into a Carolinian-shaped grammar and new open-syllable shapes (`../mare/mare.md`).

Read the Metropolitan line as the mainland clerk writes it, the Saharannaise as the Daxla
dockworker says it, the Congolaise as the Ndar market-woman says it, the Verdenese as the Portô
Venla café waiter says it — and the Meralian as the Meridian says it at home.

---

## Level 1 — formal & archival (all four spine-standards read it)

> **Metr.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Sah.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Cong.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Verd.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> *province has three regions* — "The province has three regions."

Identical in all four spine-standards. The Romance learned crust and the shared spine are the
common ground of the pluricentric language — this is why a decree drafted in any seat is legible
in the others. *(Meralian no longer shares the string: even the crust wears its open-syllable
shapes there, and the clause is built predicate-first — the daughter's constitutional register is
its own.)*

## Level 2 — one root swapped (a mainlander manages)

> **Metr.**  Markôt um sūr.
> **Sah.**  Sūg um sūr.
> **Cong.**  Lumo um sūr.
> **Verd.**  Ferjā um sūr.
> *market is big* — "The market is big."

Four markets, one skeleton. Note the Verdenese swap: *ferjā* is opaque by the letter but
half-guessable Romance — the shallowest Level 2 in the system.

## Level 3 — the divergence bites, each seat its own way

> **Metr.**  Nahtô um kylm.
> **Sah.**  Līl um barād.
> **Cong.**  Jemma um ɓūɓ.
> **Verd.**  Notē um frīu.
> *night is cold* — "The night is cold."

The domestic layer gone local three different ways — Saharan (*līl, barād*), riverine (*jemma,
ɓūɓ*, wearing the ɓ scar), austral (*notē, frīu*). The Verdenese line is Level 3a: fully
saturated, yet a mainlander half-guesses it through the Romance cousinage of the crust. The
sisters' lines stay dark.

## The daughter row — where the words themselves left home

> **Metr.**  Ajēr mä lǟdi markôtôlô. — "Yesterday I went to the market."
> **Mrl.**  *Ti lēda au.* — "I travelled (there)": the old deck particle **ti** is now the
> daughter's past, the root *lēda* the reshaped *lǟdô*, the pronoun *au* its own.

The spine-standards inflect the coursebook past *-i* whatever their vocabulary; Meralian's
ancestor register (deck Maré) leveled it to the particle *ti*, and the daughter language kept the
particle, reshaped the roots, and grew a grammar of its own (`../mare/mare.md` §3). Verdenese,
the youngest standard, took **none** of that path — the settler creole proves the full
morphology takes root fresh in new ground.

## The hybrid seam — the same machine in four hands

> **Sah.**  Žəmlôr tulē.  *(the camel-driver comes)*
> **Cong.**  Gaynôr tulē.  *(the herder comes)*
> **Verd.**  Tropôr tulē.  *(the drover comes)*

A Saharan root, a Pulaar root, a Romance root — each taking the **exact mainland suffix -ôr** and
declining like any native noun; each seat refusing the donor's own agent morpheme (*-tigi, -kat,
-oowo, -eiro, -ero, -hára*). The machine does not care which sea, which river, or which ship the
root came from. (Meralian sits this row out differently: its agents live inside its own
derivation, and even *matrūs* has grown a Meralian shape.)

## Level 3b — the land-layer (a mainlander is lost, and so are the sisters)

> **Verd.**  Jaguaretē um kaʼaguyl.
> *jaguar is in-forest* — "The jaguar is in the forest."

The copula and the case are all any other standard holds — *jaguaretē* and *kaʼaguy* (with its
puso scar) are the land's own words, gaps in Metropolitan, Saharannaise, Congolaise, and
Meralian alike, exactly as *jeli* and *wālo* are gaps everywhere but the river.

## Back-flow — the loans travel up the trunk

> **any seat:**  Matē um kalēnt.
> *mate is hot* — "The mate is hot."

As Sotoran music sent *kora* and *jeli* up the routes and the Sahara sent *gāfila*, the austral
trade sends **matē**, **tererē**, **gaūxô**, and **saudādē** north as prestige loans — each
becoming another standard's canon only by that seat's merge, never automatically. The youngest
branch feeds the trunk like every branch before it.
