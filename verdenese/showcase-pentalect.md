# Showcase — one scene, five standards

The sturdiness thesis at its fullest stretch: the same lines in **Metropolitan Nelôxi**,
**Saharannaise**, **Congolaise**, **Maré**, and **Verdenese**. The structure is identical in all
five — same word order, same copula *um*, same negation *äb*, same dozenal numbers, same object
case — so the skeleton is always transparent. What changes is the content roots (the African
standards and Verdenese), or, in exactly one licensed place, the morphology (Maré). **One spine,
five lexicons; the grammar never drifts, the vocabulary does all the diverging — and where the
grammar does bend, it bends by joint ratification, once.**

Read the Metropolitan line as the mainland clerk writes it, the Saharannaise as the Daxla
dockworker says it, the Congolaise as the Ndar market-woman says it, the Maré as the deck hand
calls it, the Verdenese as the Koinīnia café waiter says it.

---

## Level 1 — formal & archival (all five read it)

> **Metr.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Sah.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Cong.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> **Mar.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô. *(Paper Maré — full standard morphology)*
> **Verd.**  Provinsô agā tri rīgô.
> *province has three regions* — "The province has three regions."

Identical in all five. The Romance learned crust and the shared spine are the common ground of the
entire pluricentric language — this is why a decree drafted in any seat is legible in the other
four.

## Level 2 — one root swapped (a mainlander manages)

> **Metr.**  Markôt um sūr.
> **Sah.**  Sūg um sūr.
> **Cong.**  Lumo um sūr.
> **Mar.**  Markôt um sūr. *(Maré keeps the Metropolitan word-stock — that is its divergence)*
> **Verd.**  Ferjā um sūr.
> *market is big* — "The market is big."

Four markets, one skeleton — and the Maré line is the tell that its divergence lives elsewhere.
Note the Verdenese swap: *ferjā* is opaque by the letter but half-guessable Romance — the
shallowest Level 2 in the system.

## Level 3 — the divergence bites, each seat its own way

> **Metr.**  Nahtô um kylm.
> **Sah.**  Līl um barād.
> **Cong.**  Jemma um ɓūɓ.
> **Mar.**  Nahtô um kylm.
> **Verd.**  Notē um frīu.
> *night is cold* — "The night is cold."

The domestic layer gone local three different ways — Saharan (*līl, barād*), riverine (*jemma,
ɓūɓ*, wearing the ɓ scar), austral (*notē, frīu*). The Verdenese line is Level 3a: fully
saturated, yet a mainlander half-guesses it through the Romance cousinage of the crust. The
sisters' lines stay dark. Maré's stays home.

## The grammar row — where Maré diverges (and only Maré)

> **Metr.**  Ajēr mä lǟdi markôtôlô.
> **Sah.**  Ajēr mä lǟdi sūg-lô.
> **Cong.**  Ajēr mä lǟdi lumo-lô.
> **Mar.**  Ajēr mä **ti lǟdô** markôt**-lô**. *(Deck Maré: particle past, sanded case)*
> **Verd.**  Ajēr mä lǟdi ferjā-lô.
> *yesterday I went to-market* — "Yesterday I went to the market."

Four standards inflect the coursebook past *-i*; Maré alone levels it to the ratified particle
*ti* and the invariant *-lô*. Verdenese, the youngest standard, takes **none** of the sanding —
the Maritime Amendment is Maré's alone, and the settler creole proves the full morphology takes
root fresh in new ground.

## The hybrid seam — the same machine in four hands

> **Sah.**  Žəmlôr tulē.  *(the camel-driver comes)*
> **Cong.**  Gaynôr tulē.  *(the herder comes)*
> **Verd.**  Tropôr tulē.  *(the drover comes)*

A Saharan root, a Pulaar root, a Romance root — each taking the **exact mainland suffix -ôr** and
declining like any native noun; each seat refusing the donor's own agent morpheme (*-tigi, -kat,
-oowo, -eiro, -ero, -hára*). The machine does not care which sea, which river, or which ship the
root came from. (Maré sits this row out on principle: it borrows no new agent roots — its sailors
are already *matrūs*.)

## Level 3b — the land-layer (a mainlander is lost, and so are the sisters)

> **Verd.**  Jaguaretē um kaʼaguyl.
> *jaguar is in-forest* — "The jaguar is in the forest."

The copula and the case are all any other standard holds — *jaguaretē* and *kaʼaguy* (with its
puso scar) are the land's own words, gaps in Metropolitan, Saharannaise, Congolaise, and Maré
alike, exactly as *jeli* and *wālo* are gaps everywhere but the river.

## Back-flow — the loans travel up the trunk

> **any seat:**  Matē um kalēnt.
> *mate is hot* — "The mate is hot."

As Sotoran music sent *kora* and *jeli* up the routes and the Sahara sent *gāfila*, the austral
trade sends **matē**, **tererē**, **gaūxô**, and **saudādē** north as prestige loans — each
becoming another standard's canon only by that seat's merge, never automatically. The youngest
branch feeds the trunk like every branch before it.
