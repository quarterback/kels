# Verdenese — Writing Connected Prose (the composition guide)

*How to write Verdenese, not just list its words. The sisters are
`../saharannaise/composition.md` and `../congolaise/composition.md`; the metrolect's original is
`../grammar/14-composition.md`. The rule that governs everything here is the sturdiness thesis
(`verdenese.md`) under the Crisol Principle (`CRISOL-PRINCIPLE.md`): **the spine does the grammar,
the crisol does the content.** You build a Verdenese sentence exactly as a Metropolitan one — same
skeleton, same function words — and swap in the Romance root for the life you brought, the Guaraní
root for the land you found. If a mainlander can parse the structure of your sentence — and, here
uniquely, half-guess your hearth-words while going fully blind in the monte — you have written
good Verdenese.*

---

## 1 · The two halves of every sentence

- **The spine is shared and invariant** (`../saharannaise/COMMON-REGISTER.md` §1). Word order
  **SVO**; negation pre-verbal **äb**; copula **um / ūli**; past **-i** (two classes, §120);
  conditional **-ks**; modal **pidā** "must"; the case endings (object **-n**, linking **-s**,
  place **-l**, goal **-lô/-lä**, source **-lt**, comitative **-k**, partitive **-t/-tô**); the
  derivation suffixes (**-ôr, -in, -mus, -stā, -ldā, -tā, -mi**); the dozenal numbers. **You do
  not touch any of it.**
- **The content is sourced by the quarry map** (`CRISOL-PRINCIPLE.md` §1). The hearth, the table,
  the field, the feeling — the **Romance** root, from the community that owns the domain (lexicon
  H). The river, the forest, the animal, the crop the land invented — the **Guaraní** root
  (lexicon Y). The function words stay Metropolitan (*um, äb, ja, agā, tôl, bidô, pidā, -al, -l,
  -lô, -lt, -n*).

So a sentence is: **[Metropolitan grammar + function words] + [Romance hearth roots + Guaraní land
roots, by domain].**

> *Tropôr kandai tropān ñū-lô.* — "The drover brought the herd to the grassland."
> The frame (*kandai* "brought," object *-n*, goal *-lô*) is coursebook; the content (*tropā*
> [Rom.], *ñū* [Gn.]) is Verdenese — the settler's trade crossing the land's ground, which is the
> whole country in one line.

## 2 · The crisol bites — name the reality, from the right well

Three rules bite here, two shared and one unique to Verdenese:

- **The compound ban** (shared, `../saharannaise/saharannaise.md` §1). Never build a Baltic calque
  for an austral reality. The yerba is **kaʼa**, not a "green-leaf"; the capybara is **kapiʼyva**,
  not a "water-pig." Name the reality; do not translate it.
- **The land-layer line** (Verdenese's own, `CRISOL-PRINCIPLE.md` §1). Guaraní inside the land's
  domains, thickly; never outside them. The forest scene wants *kaʼaguy, jaguaretē, tatū*; the
  kitchen scene wants *kasā, latē, kexô*. A Guaraní hearth-word is as much an error as a Baltic
  calque — and so is an English word anywhere outside the western rail-and-industry layer.
- **The convergence habit.** Where the Romance cousins agree, write the fused form (*festā,
  fīlô*); where they diverge, the archived form already records which community won the domain —
  do not re-litigate it sentence by sentence.

- ✅ *Kolonôr plantai avatīn çakrāl* "the settler planted maize on the smallholding" (crop → Gn.,
  farm → Sp., agent → the seam)
- ✅ *Nonā kuinā ñoki avuī* "grandmother cooks gnocchi today" (kitchen → It.)
- ❌ *kaʼa-latē* "yerba-milk" for the mate — wrong on both counts: the drink is **matē**, and
  Guaraní does not compound into the kitchen.

## 3 · Hybrid morphology — the seam, and the near-cousin discipline

Romance or Guaraní root + mainland suffix is fully productive (`CRISOL-PRINCIPLE.md` §3). Build
agents and derived forms with the ordinary coursebook machinery — **but borrow no donor's own
morpheme**, and watch Romance especially, whose endings look almost legal beside the crust:

- agent **-ôr**: *tropā* → **tropôr** "drover" · *jerbā* → **jerbôr** "yerba-harvester" ·
  *plantā* → **plantôr** "planter" · *pan* → **panôr** "baker" · *kolôniā* → **kolonôr**
  "settler."
- The refused morphemes — Pt. *-eiro*, Sp. *-ero*, It. *-aio/-iere*, Rom. *-dor*, Gn. *-hára* —
  never enter. *tropôr*, not *tropeiro*; *jerbôr*, not *yerbatero*. Root borrowed, morpheme
  refused: that *is* the thesis.
- Verbs enter bare and conjugate as coursebook Class I: *laburā* "to work" → *laburai* "worked" ·
  *mateā* "to drink mate" → *mateai* · *çarlā* "to chat" → *çarlai*.

## 4 · Know your tier — write to the intelligibility you intend

Every sentence sits at a transparency tier (`verdenese.md` §2), and Verdenese's ladder has a step
no sister has:

- **Level 1 (all five standards read it)** — the Romance crust and the shared spine. *Provinsô
  agā tri rīgô* "the province has three regions."
- **Level 2 (a mainlander manages)** — one content root swapped: *Ferjā um sūr* "the market is
  big" (*markôt → ferjā*) — and the swap itself is half-guessable, Romance to Romance.
- **Level 3a (a mainlander guesses)** — the hearth fully saturated: *Notē um frīu* "the night is
  cold." Opaque by the letter, transparent by cousinage; the shallowest deep register in the
  system.
- **Level 3b (a mainlander is lost)** — the land-layer: *Jaguaretē um kaʼaguyl* "the jaguar is in
  the forest." The copula and nothing else. Write here when the scene leaves the settlement for
  the monte, the river, the harvest.

## 5 · A worked paragraph — the settler's spring

Every content root below is archived Verdenese (lexicons H and Y); every frame-word is coursebook.

1. *Primavērā tulī, ja kolonôr plantai avatīn çakrāl.* — "Spring came, and the settler planted
   maize on the smallholding." *(primavērā, çakrā [Rom./Sp.], avatī [Gn.]; the past and place case
   coursebook.)*
2. *Tropôr kandai tropān ñū-lô.* — "The drover brought the herd to the grassland." *(tropā
   [Rom.], ñū [Gn.]; the verb, object -n, goal -lô coursebook.)*
3. *Vespôrôl famīl·la mateai verandāl, ja çarlai.* — "In the evening the family drank mate on the
   veranda, and chatted." *(mateā, verandā, çarlā Verdenese; vespôrôl, the place -l, the past
   coursebook — famīl·la is the shared crust itself.)*
4. *Kaʼaguy ūli trankīlô, ja ñakurutū kantāi.* — "The forest was quiet, and the great owl sang."
   *(kaʼaguy, ñakurutū [Gn.], trankīlô [Rom.]; the copula past and kantāi coursebook.)*

**Run together:**

> *Primavērā tulī, ja kolonôr plantai avatīn çakrāl. Tropôr kandai tropān ñū-lô. Vespôrôl
> famīl·la mateai verandāl, ja çarlai. Kaʼaguy ūli trankīlô, ja ñakurutū kantāi.*

The skeleton never drifts — SVO, the copula, the cases, the past **-i** — and the roots of the
ships and the roots of the land decline through it alike. That is a paragraph of Verdenese:
mainland grammar, immigrant hearth, the country's own monte at the edge of the lamplight.

## 6 · When a word is missing — flag it for the Ateneô

If you need a word Verdenese does not yet have, **do not** calque it from Baltic roots and **do
not** reach across the quarry map. Flag it as a gap for the **Kēļs Ateneô** — the root is coined
by the seat from the quarry that owns the domain (§1), exactly as the coursebook coins
Metropolitan words, the Dīwān Saharan ones, and the Penc riverine ones. Shared *grammar* words are
never a Verdenese matter; those are joint, ruled by the Kolēgi with the peer seats. The gap-list
of a corpus text is its most valuable output.
