# Nelôxi Grammar — Sentence & Text Composition (from words to paragraphs)
*Foundation module 14 · the capstone: how to build a clause, join clauses, and chain sentences into
connected prose. Assumes modules 01–13. Every example uses only attested canon.*

> **Read this to WRITE.** The other modules give you the pieces (cases, verbs, postpositions,
> connectives); this one shows how to put them together into sentences and paragraphs. When you
> need something the language does not have, **flag it as a gap** (§9) — do not invent it.

## 1 · The basic clause

The skeleton is **Subject – Verb – Object**, no articles:

- *Kalāji vend kalān.* — "The fisher sells the fish." (object `-n`)

Add roles with **case endings** (module 06), not word order:

- *Kalāji vend kalān markôtôl.* — "…sells the fish **at the market**." (place `-l`)
- *Tä donā livrôn amīkôle.* — "She gives the book **to the friend**." (goal `-le` = dative)

Predication uses the one copula **um** (present) / **ūli** (past) for *is-a*, *is-like*, and
*is-at* alike:

- *Mer um sūr.* "The sea is big." · *Tä um kalāji.* "He is a fisher." · *Famīl·la um kōtôl.*
  "The family is at home." · *Mäl um kalā.* "I have a fish." (locative + *um* = "have").

## 2 · Tense & aspect inside the sentence (two levers, not verb tense)

The verb only marks **past** (`-i`); everything else rides two other levers (modules 09, 01):

- **Time-words carry futurity and sequence:** *demā* tomorrow, *nū* now, *tôl* then, *varsü* soon.
  *Demā mä lǟdô.* "Tomorrow I('ll) go."
- **The object case carries aspect:** object `-n` = done/bounded; partitive `-t/-tô` = ongoing/partial.
  *Mä lugē livrôn* "I read the book (through)" vs *Mä lugē livrôt* "I am reading a book."

So a whole narrative can run in the plain past (`-i`) with time-words doing the sequencing — you
never reach for a perfect or a progressive form.

## 3 · Joining clauses I — coordination

Chain clauses with the plain connectives (FUNCTION-WORDS):

- **ja** "and" · **agā** "but" · **või** "or" · **sest** "because" (also subordinating)
- *Tä kaptā kalān ja tulē kōju.* — "He catches the fish and comes home."
- *Mä volē lǟdô, agā tūļ um fort.* — "I want to go, but the wind is strong."

## 4 · Joining clauses II — subordination

| Relation | Word | Example |
|---|---|---|
| that (complement) | **et** | *Mä tēdô, et sä tulī.* — "I know that you came." |
| when | **kun** | *Kun tūļ langô, tä seilā.* — "When the wind drops, he sails." |
| because | **sest** | *Tä ootā, sest mer äb um vaik.* — "He waits, because the sea isn't calm." |
| if | **jōs** | *Jōs sä volē, mä tulē.* — "If you want, I('ll) come." |
| although | **kuigü** | *Kuigü tä väsinü, tä tȫdô.* — "Although he's tired, he works." |
| until (clause) | **kuni** | *Segā, kuni sup um paks.* — "Stir until the soup is thick." |
| before / after (time) | **enne / pǟrast** | *pulmôs enne* "before the wedding" (postp., noun in `-s`) |

**Relative clauses** — two strategies (module 07, VERB-REF):

- **Compact, prenominal participle** when the relative is simple: *kantāji mēs* "the singing man,"
  *donādôt livrô* "the given book."
- **Full ken/mis clause, postposed** when it carries past tense or its own object:
  *mēs, ken kantāi vespôrôl* — "the man who sang in the evening."

## 5 · Keeping track of who is who (no articles, no gender)

Because there is no *the/a* and *tä* is he/she/it alike, reference is held by **word order,
repetition, and the demonstratives**:

- **Pro-drop** once the subject is set: *Kalāji tulē. Ø Vend kalān. Ø Tulē kōju.* — the fisher stays
  the subject across all three; drop the pronoun.
- Re-introduce or switch with **se** "this/that (one)", **tä**, or by naming the noun again.
- *se* points back to the last thing mentioned: *Tä ostā kalān. Se um sūr.* "He buys the fish. It
  (that one) is big."

Rule of thumb: **name the subject when it changes, drop it when it stays.**

## 6 · Chaining sentences into a paragraph — the discourse connectives

Sequence and contrast across sentences with the discourse glue (FUNCTION-WORDS):

- **Sequence:** *esmõks* first · *tôl* then/next · *seejärõl* after that · *lopõks* finally.
- **Contrast / concession:** *omõt* "yet, all the same" · *omõtõ* "however" · *hoopõs* "instead."
- **Continuation / manner:** *niimôdõ* "this way" · *nagu* "like, as" · *ikkü* "still, after all."
- **Aspect across the story:** *jubā* "already" (perfect sense), *veel* "still," *jällü* "again."

*Esmõks tä ootā. Tôl tūļ langô. Seejärõl tä seilā. Lopõks tä tulē kōju.* — "First he waits. Then
the wind drops. After that he sails. Finally he comes home."

## 7 · A paragraph, built clause by clause

Start with bare clauses, then join and sequence them. Every word is attested canon.

1. *Matī, kalāji lǟdô merelä.* — "In the morning, the fisher goes to the sea."
2. *Tä volē kaptā kalāt, agā tūļ um fort.* — "He wants to catch fish, but the wind is strong."
   *(partitive `kalāt` = catching-fish-in-general; `agā` coordinates the contrast.)*
3. *Tä ootā, sest mer äb um vaik.* — "He waits, because the sea isn't calm." *(causal `sest`;
   negation `äb um`.)*
4. *Kun tūļ langô, tä seilā.* — "When the wind drops, he sails." *(temporal `kun`.)*
5. *Tä kaptā mult kalāt ja tulē kōju felīç.* — "He catches many fish and comes home happy."
   *(quantity `mult` + partitive; `ja` coordinates; `felīç` is a bare predicate adjective.)*

**Run together:**

> *Matī, kalāji lǟdô merelä. Tä volē kaptā kalāt, agā tūļ um fort. Tä ootā, sest mer äb um vaik.
> Kun tūļ langô, tä seilā. Tä kaptā mult kalāt ja tulē kōju felīç.*

Note the machinery working: SVO throughout, `tä` tracking the one subject (drop it or keep it),
the object case doing the aspect, time-words and `kun` doing the sequence, `agā`/`sest`/`ja`
binding the clauses — no invented forms, all canon.

## 8 · A writer's checklist

1. One clause at a time: **S – V – O**, roles by **case ending**.
2. Aspect on the **object case**, futurity/sequence on **time-words**, past on **`-i`**.
3. Join with **ja / agā / või**; subordinate with **et / kun / sest / jōs / kuigü / kuni**.
4. Relatives: participle if simple, **ken/mis** clause if it carries tense or an object.
5. Track the subject: **name it when it changes, drop it when it stays.**
6. Sequence the paragraph with **esmõks / tôl / seejärõl / lopõks**; contrast with **omõt / omõtõ**.
7. Every word checked against the headword list; **anything missing is a gap — flag it (§9).**

## 9 · Syntactic gaps that block certain moves (flag, do not invent)

Writing connected prose exposes these — a writer who hits one **flags it for the Rector**:

- **"while" (simultaneous)** — no dedicated form; *kun* "when" is the nearest. ⟨gap⟩
- **Reported speech** — direct quotation works (*Tä dī: "…"*); indirect beyond *et*-complements
  (sequence-of-tense, embedded questions) is undeveloped. ⟨open⟩
- **"no longer / anymore"** — a known gap (module 09); do not fill it.
- **Deep relative clauses** (a relative with its own past and object) — use the full **ken/mis**
  clause, not a participle.
- **Focus / clefting** ("it was the fisher who…") — no dedicated construction. ⟨open⟩

These are the frontier. The corpus texts (see `college/CORPUS-BRIEF.md`, the genre ladder) are how
we find and fill them — each new text is a probe that turns an ⟨open⟩ into a ruling.
