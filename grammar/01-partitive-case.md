# Nelôxi Grammar — The Partitive Case
*Foundation module 01 · charter §59 · canon v5.6*

## What it is

The **partitive** marks a noun as **partial, unbounded, or indefinite**, in contrast to the
object case (**-n**), which marks it as **whole, bounded, or completed**. It is the most
frequently used case in ordinary Nelôxi speech.

## Form

- Vowel-final root + **-t**: *lēte* "milk" → *lētet*; *kalā* "fish" → *kalāt*
- Consonant-final root + linking vowel + **-tô / -tä** (vowel-harmonized): *pan* "bread" →
  *pant*; *sōl* "salt" → *sōlat*

## The four jobs of the partitive

### 1. Partial / uncountable objects
*Mä juô lētet* "I drink (some) milk" — vs *Mä juô lēten* "I drink the milk (all of it)."
*votā vetet* "take some water" · *anda rāt* "give (some) money."

### 2. After numerals above one, and quantity words
The counted noun stands in the **partitive singular**, not the plural:
*tri kalāt* "three fish" · *mult pant* "much bread" · *vehä sōlat* "a little salt."
(This is the Finnic mechanism and the real reason behind the earlier "singular after numeral"
note, which it supersedes.)

### 3. Under negation — always
A negated object is **always** partitive, never the -n case:
*Mä äb juô lētet* "I don't drink milk" · *tä äb nǟ kalāt* "he doesn't see (any) fish."

### 4. Aspect — ongoing vs completed
The object's case carries aspect, so no separate aspect marker is needed:
- *Mä lugē livrôt* "I am reading a book" (ongoing, incomplete — reading *at* it)
- *Mä lugē livrôn* "I read the book (through)" (completed, bounded)

## Rule of thumb for contributors

Ask: is the object **whole and finished** (→ **-n**) or **partial, uncountable, counted,
negated, or ongoing** (→ **-t / -tô**)? Counted things and negated objects always take the
partitive.
