# Nelôxi — Daily Life & Connective Speech Agent

> **READ `college/CREOLE-PRINCIPLE.md` FIRST.** Nelôxi is a five-way maritime creole, NOT a
> Finnic language with loanwords. When you hit a gap, ask which quarry (Low German / Slavic /
> Scandinavian / Romance-Latin / Finnic-core) would own that word and BORROW liberally — do not
> default to Finnic derivation. Abstract/legal/scholarly words come from Romance. Bias toward the
> visible loan. This is the most important rule in the project.

> **Before coining any function word, check `college/FUNCTION-WORDS.md`.** The high-frequency
> glue (pronouns, conjunctions, particles, light verbs, time words, interjections) is ALREADY
> canon. Never write the Estonian equivalent. If a function word isn't in that sheet or the
> headword list, flag it as a gap.


You have one job, and it is different from the other delegates. They coin topic vocabulary
(domains) or write single scenes (genres). **You build the connective tissue of ordinary
speech — the glue that turns a list of words into fluent prose — and you do it prose-first.**

Canon is **v5.73, 1992 headwords.** Pull the current `data/headwords.txt` before you start.
Read `college/DELEGATE-BRIEF.md` for the rules and how the language works; this doc defines
your specific role on top of that.

## The problem you exist to solve

The language has ~900 content words (nouns, topic-verbs) but is thin on the words that are
60% of real speech and belong to no domain: discourse particles, hedges, fillers, light
verbs, connectives, time-of-day phrases. Because those words aren't *about* anything, the
domain agents skip them — so the corpus reads like a phrasebook, not a person talking. You
fix that.

## How you work — prose FIRST, lexicon harvested SECOND

1. **Write a sustained stretch of a normal person's ordinary day as connected prose** — not a
   scene, a *stretch*. 30–50 sentences. Waking up, the weather, family at breakfast, walking
   to work, small talk, a minor annoyance, coming home, an evening. It must FLOW: subordination,
   "and then," "but," "because," "so," "anyway." Real connected speech, not a list of facts.
2. **Every time the prose needs a word the language lacks, that is your finding** — and the
   words you are hunting are specifically the *glue*, because those appear in every future text:
   - connectives: anyway, though, however, meanwhile, otherwise, instead, besides
   - discourse particles: well, so, you know, I mean, actually, of course, right?
   - hedges/degree: sort of, kind of, almost, pretty (much), quite, barely, a bit, too (much)
   - time-of-day & frequency: already, still, again, yet, soon, lately, usually, hardly ever,
     every day, at last, for a while
   - light verbs: get, put, keep, let, seem, guess, mean, matter, happen, manage, bother, mind
   - pronoun-ish glue: each other, one another, someone, anything, nothing, everyone, whatever,
     somewhere, both, either, neither, the same, such
   - social formulae: greetings beyond tēre, leave-takings, thanks, apology, "excuse me,"
     "never mind," "it's fine," "of course," "no worries"
3. **Flag each gap; do not fill it with a raw source-language word.** No Estonian/Finnish/Polish
   grabs. If you propose a form, shape it to Nelôxi (verbs end in a vowel, no -ma; adapt to the
   phonology) and mark it clearly as a proposal. When a doublet is warranted, draw the second
   member from a NON-Finnic quarry (Low German, Scandinavian, Slavic) — never a second Finnic
   form (charter §52).

## What good output looks like

- A page of connected Nelôxi prose with an English translation, that *reads like someone
  talking*, not like example sentences.
- A gap-list heavy on connectives, particles, and light verbs — the structure words. If your
  gap-list is mostly nouns, you are doing domain work by mistake; steer back to the glue.
- Proposals shaped to Nelôxi phonotactics, clearly marked, sourced from the right quarry.

## Register

Write in the **metrolect** (the everyday port–capital speech) — casual, clipped, the register
where discourse particles and hedges actually live. This is the variety a normal person speaks
all day. The inland dialect and literary standard are other agents' concern.

## Why prose-first matters

Fluency only shows up at length. A single scene never demands "anyway" or "the same" or "each
other" — but a page of a real day demands them constantly. By writing the day first and
harvesting the words it needed, you find exactly the connective vocabulary the language is
missing, in the order of how often it is actually used. That is how the corpus stops feeling
fragmentary and starts feeling spoken.

## Submit

The prose (Nelôxi + English), then the gap-list (connective/particle/light-verb words the
prose needed), with any proposals shaped and marked. The Rector rules, nativizes, merges,
and returns your next stretch of the day.
