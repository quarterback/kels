# Nelôxi — The Metaphor Fields (how the language carries its culture)

*The coining doctrine and the cultural soil it grows from. Read this before coining any **abstract**
or **culture-bearing** word. It exists because a language is not a cipher: you do not name "dignity"
by shaving the ending off Latin *dignitas*. You name it the way this place — a cold maritime,
trade-honor, border-parish coast — already lives it. The corpus already does this in places; this
doc makes the method explicit so every future word has feel.*

---

## The doctrine (charter §122)

**For abstract and culture-bearing concepts, coin by native metaphor from Nelôxian material life —
compound or extend native roots. Reserve loans for genuinely imported institutions.**

- **Build, don't borrow, for what the culture already lives.** Dignity, conscience, fairness,
  kinship, grief, courage, shame — a fishing-and-trading people had these long before any Latin
  arrived. Coin them from the sea, the salt, the scale, the net, the black-book, the hearth.
- **Borrow only for what genuinely arrived from outside**, and let it keep its outside name: the
  church (Latin/Greek — *episkopô, eukaristī, sakramentô*), bureaucracy and law (Romance —
  *federatsiôn, prokurôr, delegatsiôn*), sport and tech (international — *golf, failô, tennis*),
  the counting-house and the wharf (Low German — *markôt, pundô, tonnô*).
- **The test:** *would a fisher-trader on this coast reach for this image?* If the coin is a foreign
  word plus a landing vowel for a concept the culture already owns, it **fails** — rebuild it from
  the material life. A word should carry a picture, not a translation.

This refines §79 (borrow from the owning quarry): the *owning quarry of an abstraction the culture
already lives is the culture itself*, not a European language.

## The fields (the soil — each grounded in words already in canon)

### 1 · The sea gives and takes — fate, risk, loss, the turning of luck
The founding proverb is *Mer donā, ja mer votā* "The sea gives, and the sea takes." Loss and fortune
are tidal, not earned. Already here: **rāndduhô** "shore spirit, spirit of drowned sailors,"
**laintū** "the swell, the great wave," **lūdô** "tide." Reach here for: fortune, ruin, the sudden
reversal, mortality.

### 2 · The salt & the scale — worth, honor, fairness, justice
Trade-honor is the moral spine of a port. Worth is weighed; fairness is the balance holding level.
Already here: **vāgô** "balance, scale," **pundô** "weight," **empatô** "the *earned, honorable*
draw." New from this field: **nimpundô** "dignity — the weight a person's *name* carries,"
**ühüvāgô** "equal — of one weight, level on the scale." Reach here for: dignity, fairness, equality,
reputation, debt-of-honor.

### 3 · The black-book — debt, sin, reckoning, conscience
**mustlivrô** "the black book — the parish debt/scandal/excommunication register" is a real cultural
object: the town keeps an account against you. So conscience is the account you keep against
*yourself*: **isülivrô** "conscience — the inner ledger, the black-book on oneself." Reach here for:
guilt, obligation, the private reckoning, memory-as-debt.

### 4 · The net & the boat — shared labor, crew, kinship, common lot
Kinship on this coast is who pulls the same net, not a bloodline abstraction. Already here: **vôrk**
"net," **ühü** "shared, common," **bōt** "boat." New: **ühüvôrk** "brotherhood, fellowship — those
who pull one net." Reach here for: solidarity, belonging, the shared burden, the crew.

### 5 · The hearth-fire — home, belonging, warmth, the domestic
**fôk** "the hearth-fire, the domestic fire," **āhi** "oven," **peçô** "the great interior stove,"
**vanamǟr** "grandmother." Reach here for: home, welcome, comfort, the inside vs. the cold outside.

### 6 · The parish & the bell — folk religion, death, fear, the border rite
A liminal frontier of mixed Latin/Orthodox rite. **fānkellô** "the death-bell rung for plague,
wreck, or execution," **paterbabkô** "prayer-grandmother, paid to mourn the dead," **pīrôritô** "the
mixed border rite." Reach here for: dread, mourning, the uncanny, sworn oaths.

### 7 · The pitch & the terrace — the modern press register (already the richest field)
The football/press vocabulary is where the corpus already sings, and it is the *model* for the rest:
**langômus** "the bleed — a lead draining away slowly," **luxô** "the gifted passenger, all touch and
no work," **fosô** "the quiet capitulation — a side that stops believing without drama," **untô**
"the lull — the dead stretch both sides feel," **trotsô** "defiant grit — effort past the body's
limit," **klȫtôr** "the player with nerve, who wants the ball when it is hardest." Every one is an
*image with an attitude*. That is the target for all coining, not just sport.

## One design consequence worth stating

**There is no generic "to make / do."** You *cook* soup (**kuinā**), *build* a boat (**ehitā**),
*ready* a meal (**valmistā**), *work* a field (**tȫdô**), *mend* a net (**parandā**). The language
makes you name the specific act. That is not a gap to fill with a light verb — it is the culture
refusing an empty word. (An unregistered generic *tek*- once leaked into a text; it was corrected,
not canonized.)

## The pass-back (§123) — auditing the abstract register for relex

A sweep of the emotion/virtue/social-abstraction vocabulary, applying the test above. The result is
reassuring and instructive:

- **The emotion field grew up native.** shame *häbū*, joy *ilo*, grief *leinô* / *leinā*, fear
  *pōr* / *pelgô*, pride *ūpū* / *ūpeldā*, longing *iglū*, hope *lōtū* / *lōtā*, memory *mēl*, calm
  *rahu*, worry *mūrô*, endurance *kannômus*, trust *usaldā*, love-verb *ārmastô*, affection *arm* —
  all native. Courage rides the body: *klȫtô* "balls → nerve." Nothing to fix here.
- **One clear offender, reworked:** *fatômô* "fate" (Latin *fatum*) → **merjagô** "the share the sea
  deals you" (the sea-field). *fatômô* stays as the literary doublet.
- **The native–loan doublets are a feature, not a bug.** *amōr* (elevated love) beside native *arm*
  / *ārmastô*; *libertā* (legal freedom) beside the open-passage sense of *līberü*; *animô* (church
  soul) beside folk *duhô* / *navô*. English has exactly this (freedom/liberty, deep/profound). A
  loan is a relex **sin only when it is the *sole* word** for something the culture lives.
- **Lived freedom got an *idiom*, not a word (§124).** *Säs eläji kōrrô karrēl ja menjā sigôkarnt*
  "your animals are running in the street eating ham" — freedom you can see, the yard thrown open.
  See `reader/idioms.md`. This is the pattern to reach for when a concept wants a *scene*, not a
  headword — the fullest texture there is.

The lesson: the corpus was healthier than it looked — the relex was concentrated in the *most
abstract, latest-coined* layer (the UDHR cluster, fate), precisely where a coiner reaches for Latin
because the concept feels "academic." That instinct is the thing to resist.

## For contributors

When you hit an abstract concept and no word exists:
1. Ask which **field** above it belongs to (sea? scale? black-book? net? hearth? parish? pitch?).
2. Coin from that field's **native roots**, by compound or extension — give it a *picture*.
3. Write the gloss as an *image with a stance*, like the pitch words — not a bare dictionary
   equivalent. "dignity — the weight a name carries" beats "dignity (n.)".
4. Only if the concept genuinely arrived from outside (an institution, a technology) do you borrow —
   and then from its owning quarry (§79), keeping its outside name.
